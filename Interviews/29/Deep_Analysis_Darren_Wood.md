### 2. Inventory Management Paradox üì¶

#### What Darren Said:
> "You got two trends to fight. You got some customers that know the need to hold critical spares, they're in the middle of nowhere... And some of them are very forward thinking, like we're gonna hold these spares, but there's others that don't. And there's two reasons for that. It's, I don't wanna say they're not that organised, but that is the case sometimes. **And some of it is because of cost, like they roll the dice.**"

#### Critical Insights:
1. **Customer segmentation**:
   - **Forward-thinking customers**: Stock critical spares (understand the risk)
   - **Cost-conscious customers**: "Roll the dice" (gamble on uptime)
   - **Disorganized customers**: Don't track inventory properly

2. **Distributor challenges**:
   - **What to stock?**: "One of things we struggle with is what should we keep in stock and why should we keep it in stock?"
   - **Component compatibility**: "If I have a contactor, for example, for starting a motor, then I need the auxiliary contact box. I need the overload with it"
   - **Wrong stock problem**: "You can have parts which don't make sense together. And because of that stops you from selling those components."

3. **Purchasing disconnect**:
   - **Non-technical buyers**: "Your purchasing group that might not be technically orientated and they go in and they order parts based on, you know, numerical system"
   - **Missing complementary parts**: "They don't realize they should have bought this as well. And as a result of that, that part that they got doesn't work, and the part that they need to work with this, oh, is now on a four week delivery."

#### Strategic Implications:
- ‚úÖ **Validates Kwadwo's working capital hypothesis**: Cost-conscious customers are gambling to avoid inventory costs
- üÜï **New opportunity**: **AI-driven inventory optimization** - help customers and distributors stock the RIGHT parts, not just MORE parts
- üÜï **Ecosystem insight**: Distributors are ALSO struggling with inventory decisions - they're potential partners, not just competitors

### 3. Lead Time & Logistics Breakdown ‚è±Ô∏è

#### What Darren Said:

#### Critical Insights:

   - **Human error**: "You got that human element and that, that's where it goes wrong"
   - **Vendor coordination**: "You physically have to pick up on call, because now it's gone from our system to their system"
   - **Personal intervention**: "If I have issues with my customer, I physically have to do that calling, and it's a shame, but I can't rely on anybody else"

3. **Real-world example**:
   - **Central Newfoundland motor failure**: Customer advised to stock spare, declined, motor failed, **10-day emergency delivery**
   - **Result**: Darren created "complete components list for them of spares" (reactive, not proactive)

#### Quantitative Data:
- **Standard ground delivery**: 5 days (Newfoundland from Ontario/Quebec)
- **Standard air delivery**: Next day (if in stock)
- **Emergency delivery (real example)**: **10 days** (motor, Central Newfoundland)
- **Vendor coordination time**: Unquantified, but "takes human beings to go through it"

#### Strategic Implications:
- ‚úÖ **Validates emergency logistics pain**: Even with air freight, 10-day delays happen
- üÜï **Human error is a major failure point**: Automation exists, but rush orders bypass it
- üÜï **Darren personally intervenes**: He can't rely on his own team for critical orders - this is a HUGE pain point

### 4. ERP Integration - Partial Success, Major Gaps üíª

#### What Darren Said:
> "We've got access to their stocking systems, okay, and there is a, there is an integration between them and us... But we only see their Canadian stock and also the US stock. But it's all the stuff for Trump at the moment. That's a little bit like, Christ I, that that is, that is out at..."

> "Where it becomes tangly though is manufacturers stock. **If it's not stock on their shelf and it has to be built and it has to be made. #### Critical Insights:

   - **Long delivery items**: "That's where that work then begins to take time"
   - **No predictive visibility**: System doesn't warn customers about obsolescence or long lead times

#### Strategic Implications:
- ‚úÖ **ERP integration exists**: This is NOT a greenfield problem - systems are in place
- üÜï **Gap is in build-to-order parts**: Standard catalog parts work fine; custom/long-lead parts are the problem

### 5. Additive Manufacturing Reaction - Cautiously Optimistic ‚úÖ

#### What Darren Said:
> "I think this definitely, cause I gonna, I gonna get you some interesting, cause, I mean like, with component parts. I think it's, um, **that could make sense.** So you building a machine. There's parts that we have, like, we have to go out for that needs to be fabricated, that needs to be made. **I bet that's what takes us time.** If we're building something, is that fab duck pass, and they are, and all the guys in our value ad team are looking at that. **They're looking at additive manufacturing.**"

> "I had a tool made up for to put on the end of a robot, and was the the the, the Dell ring which the, you know, the Teflon material on the end, which actually took the longest to get that machined. **Like, if you got a 3D printed that, like, you could have made that part**, and whether it gave us the finish or not, but maybe it does. #### Critical Insights:
1. **Use case validation**:
   - **Custom fabricated parts**: "That needs to be fabricated, that needs to be made. I bet that's what takes us time."
   - **Robot end-effector tooling**: Teflon/Delrin ring - "took the longest to get that machined"
   - **R&D prototyping**: "If we do an R&D projects, that's where it really comes into its own making those parts"
   - **One-offs**: "Who's making one offs? Maybe, like, I need to do, I'm testing a machine, so I need to make a prototype of that part."

2. **Limitations acknowledged**:
   - **Electrical components**: "We're doing manufactured parts, so, like, it's a terminal block. It's a, it's a variable speed drive. Like, you'd only be able to print the housing"
   - **Material constraints**: "What could we make it with, like, it's like in, in, uh, aluminum extrusion"
   - **Realistic assessment**: "We're in a hard business for 3D printing. We are cause there's some parts sure, yeah"

3. **Team interest**:
   - **Value-add team**: "All the guys in our value ad team are looking at that. They're looking at additive manufacturing."
   - **Active exploration**: Proax is ALREADY investigating AM for custom parts

#### Strategic Implications:
- ‚úÖ **Validates AM for custom fabricated parts**: This is the sweet spot
- ‚úÖ **Validates R&D/prototyping use case**: One-offs, machine testing, custom tooling
- ‚ùå **Electrical components are NOT the target**: Can't print terminal blocks or variable speed drives (only housings)
- üÜï **Proax is already exploring AM**: They're not skeptics - they're actively looking for solutions
