# Interview Transcript — Jared Newcombe (Interview #65)
**Interviewee:** Jared Newcombe  
**Position:** Chief Operating Officer, Horizon Naval Engineering  
**Date:** [Date of Interview]  
**Interviewer:** Mahmoud Kiasari

---

## TRANSCRIPT

**Mahmoud:** Thank you for being here today. I know your schedule is busy, so I appreciate your time. I'm doing my PhD at Dalhousie University and about 2 years ago, we developed an idea to address supply chain management problems using additive manufacturing. I was recently accepted into the Lab2Market program, which requires me to interview at least 100 experts to validate my idea. Would it be okay if you introduced yourself first?

**Jared:** Sure. I have about 30–38 years now in shipbuilding and ship repair. I spent 24 years at Irving Shipbuilding, 6 years at Davie Shipbuilding, and another 5 years at Ontario Shipyards. Now I'm back on the East Coast working with Horizon Naval Engineering, who own part of NewDock. So I have a lot of work to do down there as well.

**Mahmoud:** That's excellent. So I have a few structured questions I'd like to walk through. My first question: Over your 30 years managing programs ranging from $100M to over $1B, what are the true cost drivers that shock stakeholders? Is it materials, labor, rework, scheduled delays, or something else?

**Jared:** Materials and subcontractors are generally not the big issue—they're fixed with limited variability. The largest variable is **labor efficiency**, which typically makes up 60% of the contract. It fluctuates between 60–40 depending on whether it's a new build or repair. New builds are 60% materials (you buy engines, motors, etc.), but repair jobs flip that because you're repairing items purchased during the original build.

**Contractual terms** can introduce variability and cost, but there are also provisions that can be impactful to the project as a whole. The **client** is often a variable—their quality expectations. If the quality standard isn't clearly defined in the contract, shipyards provide the bare minimum because everyone's competitive. So everyone quotes the low standard, then negotiates once the client is on-site about what the minimum actually is. That's typically where **change orders** happen.

In a repair scenario, you open up a valve or tank and find there's a lot more work than originally scoped. Then you negotiate with the owner on cost and schedule impact.

For new builds, change orders typically result from engineering design changes. If the ship owner decides during engineering they want a bigger engine or different valve configuration, that becomes a change order. We negotiate the schedule and cost impact.

**Mahmoud:** That makes sense. My next question: At Ontario Shipyards, you managed suppliers ranging from $100K to $20M agreements. In a typical multi-year program with hundreds of suppliers, how do you track whether a critical supplier is at risk of delay, quality failure, or cost overrun?

**Jared:** I'll break it down into two pieces. First, **management structure**: It's never a single person. While I head up procurement, solicitation, or estimating, there's a whole team—a buyer responsible for 10 suppliers, beneath that a project manager, operations manager, and on-site supervisor dealing with suppliers daily.

**For schedule tracking:** We define the work breakdown structure and subcontractor portions upfront. The subcontractor provides input on what they can do. Let's say they commit to 6 months. Every week, they update: "I'm 2% done, 5% done, 10% done." The schedule extrapolates—if current progress continues, the end date might be 9 months, not 6. We look for **patterns over several weeks or a month or two**, not individual weeks. Once we build a case that they won't be on time, we manage them through meetings, bring in more people, or take work away.

**For cost tracking:** Cost variance typically comes from change orders. For example, a painting subcontractor quotes $100K to paint 10,000 sq ft in a tank. They discover the tank is actually 15,000 sq ft. They submit a change order for the additional 5,000 sq ft. I go to the client and negotiate: "Do you want the extra 5,000 sq ft painted?" Yes or no. Based on that, we adjust the contract.

**Mahmoud:** Where do you lose the most time in procurement—defining specs, comparing bids, contract review, or something else?

**Jared:** **The majority of time is lost on the front end.** Typically, a client comes out with a spec and asks for quotes. We bid and, if we win, the time between contract award and vessel arrival is very short. So we have a lot of procurement to do in limited time with limited resources.

We focus on **critical path items first**, then expand to other paths with varying degrees of float. We buy in that sequence. There's usually a window because the first part of the job is strip-out and cutouts—several weeks in before we need to install steel or motors.

**Finding properly specced material** is where we see the most impact on the front end. Material has to match the work requirements exactly.

As you work through a contract and find change orders, it interrupts the flow. A team of 5–6 people working on a scope has to stop. I might need to buy something, negotiate with the client on cost and schedule, then procure the material—which could be a week or two, maybe the next day. That delay means I then have to redirect those 5–6 people to new work. There's lots of interruption like that.

In repair, this is constant. New builds don't have this issue as much.

**Mahmoud:** My final question: When facing critical decisions—whether to bring on a backup supplier, accelerate procurement, or adjust the schedule—what information do you wish you had that you don't have today?

**Jared:** To reframe your question: **The information is all there; it's readily available.** If a vendor isn't performing, I have schedule indicators showing they're slipping. I know other vendors who can supplement or replace them.

**The most important piece is clear, open communication from the supplier.** Suppliers don't want to say they're behind schedule, they don't have enough people, they didn't order paint in time. That's what causes problems. You typically get excuses instead of "here's our plan to get back on track."

I'll give you an example: On a Terry ship, we had Oglama Painting doing $8M of work. Within 3 months, they were grossly behind schedule. Despite numerous meetings, lack of communication, refusal to bring more people in—just excuse after excuse. I ultimately replaced them. I brought them in one day and said, "You're done. Pack your gear. We're bringing someone else in."

**The information I'm not missing is clear communication from the supplier and their willingness to work with me to solve problems.** At the end of the day, I've spent time and effort issuing a contract. The most dire thing I can do is cancel it at 10–20% done. They've mobilized, bought paint and equipment, brought people in. Cancellation is catastrophic—I have to pay another vendor to mobilize and buy paint again. It's more cost-effective to continue, even at higher cost, than to start over.

**One improvement area:** Even though we have early schedule indicators showing someone's behind, there's variation in how progress is measured. Someone might report "I think I'm 10% done," but where's the evidence? It's subjective vs. objective. When you need to install 100 widgets and you've installed 20, you're 20% done—that's clear. But painting 10,000 sq ft with blast, prime, prep, and coats becomes subjective.

**We typically look for 3–4 weeks of bad numbers to confirm a trend and say we have a problem.** We'd like to solidify that confidence earlier without waiting that long.

**Mahmoud:** One quick follow-up: You mentioned a lack of clear communication. Have you considered using AI or software tools to manage these problematic areas and reduce issues before they escalate?

**Jared:** I don't think any single person is an expert in almost anything. I used to think I was fairly competent in my immunity, but I use ChatGPT quite a bit and it allows different thought processes to come into play and think about things from different perspectives. I'm wide open to the use of AI and how it can support our industry.

One of our mandates is to look at AI and how we can bring it in responsibly to help our systems—whether that's planning, scheduling, procurement, you name it. Everything's on the table as to how we can move forward and make life easier.

**Mahmoud:** Great. The platform I'm working on includes digital inventory management using AI. Would you be interested in staying connected as I develop this further?

**Jared:** Yes, absolutely. I'd be happy to continue the conversation and support your development.

## KEY THEMES IDENTIFIED

1. **Labor efficiency is the largest variable cost driver** (60% of contracts)
2. **Quality ambiguity** creates change orders and cost escalation
3. **Supplier communication gaps** are the primary operational risk
4. **Progress measurement is subjective** and lacks early warning precision
5. **Procurement front-end bottlenecks** occur when specs aren't clear
6. **Schedule tracking through weekly updates** provides early signals but requires 3–4 weeks of data to act
7. **Openness to AI** for planning, scheduling, and procurement optimization
