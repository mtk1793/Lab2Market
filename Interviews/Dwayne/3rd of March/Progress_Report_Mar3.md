## Key Finding

**Uncovered:** The real barrier to AM adoption at enterprise scale is not supply chain visibility or printing capability—it's **DRL 3 (Digital Readiness Level) certification scaling economics**. Operators and classification societies validate that certification cost ($10k–$50k per part) blocks adoption unless amortized across 100+ customers. Pre-certified parts library is the unlock.

**Evidence:**
- Lloyd's Register (Adam Saxty) provided complete 7-step AM certification pathway: facility qualification done ONCE, design qualification done ONCE per part, only production verification required per order.
- ExxonMobil (Craig Dicken) confirmed: "How do you scale digital readiness affordably? Getting to DRL 3 is not easy... you do not have to spend a disproportionate amount of money getting that image ready."
- Aker Solutions has formal AM screening process globally; confirmed certification workflow exists but not formalized with Atlantic XL (NL) yet.
- Equinor validates spare parts represent 1–2% capex but protect 98% production value; critical parts have 10–18 month lead times.
- Horizon Maritime confirms $50k–$100k/day downtime cost from missing parts.
- Supply chain researcher (Ali) validates 10–15% ROI minimum threshold for technology adoption across industrial supply chains.

**Pattern:** Operators understand AM value proposition (faster parts, lower inventory) but certification/qualification cost blocks adoption. Classification societies have structured pathways but no mechanism to amortize certification across operators. Each operator certifies parts independently—duplicating $10k–$50k cost per part family.

**Contradiction:** ExxonMobil ($400B+ company) lacks cross-region parts visibility despite massive IT infrastructure. Manual intervention required due to SAP/TM/EWM system fragmentation. Even sophisticated operators run "last-minute" reactive MRO supply chains.

**Surprise:** OEMs may PREFER not managing spare parts (Sheila Paterson insight)—it's non-core burden for them. Reframes OEM relationship from "skeptic who must be convinced" to "willing licensor seeking revenue without operational overhead."

**Why it matters:** This reframes entire platform positioning from "digital inventory for supply chain visibility" to **"DRL 3 Certification-as-a-Service + Pre-Certified Parts Library."** The defensible moat is not software—it's certified parts library (data asset) + Lloyd's Register partnership (regulatory authority) + Atlantic XL facility (first Lloyd's-certified AM in Americas).

**Implication:** Platform value is in **certification orchestration**, not inventory dashboards. Integration strategy must focus on systems-of-systems connectivity (SAP/TM/EWM/vendor systems) rather than standalone software. Maritime operators (vessels) are better first target than offshore platforms due to lower certification barriers (DNV certifies at vessel/equipment level, not individual parts).
