# Interview 59: Deep Strategic Insights — Digital Inventory for Advanced Manufacturing -  Evan Butler-Jones


**Interview Subject:** Senior Vice-President, Product & Strategy (ecospheres); 6+ years in additive manufacturing; prior: welding innovation, aviation maintenance/software

**Thesis:** The path to scaling a digital-inventory + distributed-print platform is not through building a generalized "platform" first, but through solving one operator's acute downtime problem with one print partner, then weaponizing that case study to climb the risk-aversion ladder.

---

## Core Strategic Tensions & Resolutions

### 1. **The Problem-First Imperative: Why 90% of Lab Innovations Fail**

The interviewee's fundamental insight: most lab innovations are **solutions looking for problems**, not problems looking for solutions. This is critical for the founder's mindset shift.

**The current risk:** Michaelwas approaching his platform as "we have AM + digital inventory + AI + ecosystem orchestration." The expert's perspective: *start by fixing one specific, high-cost customer pain (e.g., offshore platform shutdown due to critical part failure). Then retrofit the platform concept around that.*

**Deep implication:** The founder's business model canvas likely lists "reduce downtime cost" as a value prop, but it's abstract. The validation question is: *which operator, on which part family, in which geography, with what current failure rate and $-per-hour downtime, and at what dollar threshold does a 48-hour print + ship solve the problem?*

This must be answered before pitching AI, digital inventory, or ecosystem orchestration.

---

### 2. **Economic Justification Beats Innovation Sexiness**

The expert repeatedly stressed: **"Your competition is every other innovation project that company has on the books."**

Meaning: an oil & gas operator weighs your digital AM platform against:
- Buying a second CNC (100% proven, known cost, known payback).
- Increasing spare parts inventory at a hub location.
- Paying a premium for expedited shipping from an OEM.
- Accepting planned downtime windows.

**The delta calculation must be brutal:**
- Current cost of one critical failure (e.g., $ Mio for an offshore shutdown): ✓ known.
- Probability of failure per year: ✓ known.
- Cost to mitigate today (inventory, expedited OEM parts, planned downtime windows): ✓ Known.
- Your cost to print + deliver that part locally: ✓ must be quantified.
- Confidence discount: ✓ operator will discount your unproven solution 40–50% vs. a known vendor.

**Actionable:** Before pitching, build a financial model with breakeven at 1 failure mitigated per year. Then go to 2–3 operators with similar parts and validate the math.

---

### 3. **Integration & Process Change: Where Scaling Actually Stalls**

The expert's major theme: *technical feasibility is the smallest problem. Integration into existing processes is where projects die.*

**What this means for digital inventory:**
- Printing a part successfully in a lab ≠ printing it repeatedly with consistent specs for critical use.
- The operator's QA department must validate the part (material certs, process validation, dimensional inspection, functional testing).
- The part may not fit into existing supply-chain documentation, procurement workflows, or regulatory compliance frameworks.
- Operators have change-control boards; adding a new print vendor + digital TDP requires board approval, training, and process documentation.

**Deep challenge:** If the part is mission-critical (offshore, defense, aerospace), the qualification bar is *extremely high*. The expert stated: **"even if you got aerospace qualified, it would be unusual… the qualification standards are so robust."**

**Resolution:** Start with non-mission-critical or less-regulated spare parts (e.g., enclosures, brackets, flow housings). Win those cases. Then incrementally move into higher-assurance parts once you've built trust and process muscle.

---

### 4. **IP & Contracting: The Real Barrier to Entry**

The CEO framed IP mitigation smartly: encrypted escrow, per-print licensing, revenue share to OEMs. The expert's response: **smart, but insufficient without major contracting clarity.**

**The deep issue:**
- OEMs profit from aftermarket spare-parts revenue. They will *resist* allowing operators to print spare parts, even if encrypted and licensed.
- If an OEM's valve or pump fails, the operator traditionally buys replacement from OEM at a 40–60% markup.
- A digital inventory allowing local printing erodes that margin.
- OEMs will have legal/business teams block participation *unless* there's a financial incentive (e.g., rev-share) AND contractual clarity on liability, warranties, and IP ownership.

**Why this is solvable (but hard):**
- For parts where the OEM *no longer manufactures* (legacy equipment, end-of-life parts), IP resistance is moot. The OEM already abandoned the market.
- For parts where OEMs *do* manufacture, the rev-share model can work *IF* you convince them that: (a) they make some margin on each print; (b) they avoid warehousing costs; (c) they capture higher customer loyalty.

**Tactical implication:** Start the platform with "orphaned" parts (no longer sold by OEMs). Build case studies. Then approach OEMs from a position of demonstrated demand, not theoretical pitch.

---

### 5. **Geography & Scale: Why Canada Alone Is Underscaled**

The expert's verdict: **"Unless you get a bunch of money from DND to develop this for DND, I don't see another operator of equipment at a scale big enough in Canada that could jump through those hurdles."**

This is candid & important. Canada has:
- Mid-sized oil & gas operators (large in global terms, but smaller than Saudi Aramco or Shell).
- Limited aerospace tier-1 players (Bombardier, Airbus Canada).
- No major automotive OEMs relative to US / EU.
- DND/DLA as a consolidated buyer (actually a *feature*, not a bug).

**The scaling math:**
- Your platform needs 10–20 operators with similar parts and pain points to achieve network effects.
- Canada has maybe 3–5 in any single sector (oil & gas, defense, aerospace).
- To get density, you *must* expand to the US or include international operators.

**But the expert also noted:** DND and DLA-like government programs *are* consolidating buyers with high mission-criticality and high tolerance for new solutions. **This is your lever.**

**Tactical direction:** Pitch to DND first (or equivalents: DARPA, NRC-IRAP), not to a commercial operator. Government funding + DND pilot = national resilience angle, justified R&D spend, and de-risking for future commercial rollout.

---

### 6. **De-Risking & Pilot Motion: The Recursive Climb**

The expert outlined a clear de-risking ladder:
1. **Tier 0:** Lab proof (you already have this).
2. **Tier 1:** Pilot customer (innovation center, government program, or risk-tolerant SME).
3. **Tier 2:** Case study + testimonial (operator on record, measurable ROI).
4. **Tier 3:** Replication pilots (same solution, different operator/part).
5. **Tier 4:** Ecosystem adoption (multiple vendors, operators, and parts).

**Why this matters:** Investors and customers use Tier 2–3 outcomes to justify risk. You're currently at Tier 0–1. The path forward is *not* to build the full platform first; it's to **land a Tier-1-2 pilot, instrument it for measurable outcomes, and then repeat.**

**Measurable outcomes for a pilot (from the expert's language):**
- Mean time to repair (MTTR) reduction.
- Downtime hours avoided (and $ value).
- Print lead-time vs. OEM lead-time.
- Part cost per print vs. OEM list price.
- Operator confidence/satisfaction (NPS or similar).

**A single pilot with clear ROI is worth 100 PowerPoint slides.**

---

### 7. **The Organizational Sponsorship Requirement**

The expert stressed: **"Leadership sponsorship is critical. Whenever you make a change, some things are going to get worse, some things are going to get better."**

This is about change management, not just technology. If you're asking an offshore platform to adopt a new parts-supply workflow, you're asking them to:
- Retrain operations staff.
- Update their supply-chain procedures and compliance audits.
- Accept new vendor/print-provider reliability risk.
- Change their procurement authorization workflows.

**This requires a sponsor inside the operator** — someone with P&L authority and the political capital to absorb short-term friction for long-term benefit.

**Implication for customer discovery:**
- Don't pitch the VP of Operations directly; identify the sponsor (VP of Asset Integrity, Chief Engineer, or Head of Supply Chain).
- Understand their incentives: do they own uptime KPIs, cost-reduction targets, or supply-chain resilience?
- Build a business case anchored to *their* goals, not yours.

## Synthesis: A Revised Go-to-Market Strategy

Based on this expert's experience, here's the **high-conviction path forward:**

### Phase 1: Single-Operator MVP (Weeks 1–12)
1. **Identify one operator** with a high-impact spare part and documented downtime costs (target: $ 0.5–5M per failure; failure rate ≥ 1x/year).
2. **Select part criteria:** non-mission-critical (to avoid aerospace-level qualification), but high-enough impact to justify new procurement process.
3. **Secure one print provider** in that geography (or co-located); vet for quality & lead-time.
4. **Design IP-safe workflows:** encrypted TDP escrow, audit-level tracking, and revenue-share terms for any OEM involvement.
5. **Deliver measurable outcomes:** MTTR reduction, downtime avoided, cost per part, operator satisfaction.

### Phase 2: Case Study & Funding (Weeks 12–24)
1. **Publish pilot ROI:** quantified outcomes from Phase 1.
2. **Pursue government funding:** NRC-IRAP, DND innovation grants, or DLA-adjacent programs. Position as "national supply-chain resilience" or "operational sustainability."
3. **De-risk via replication:** identify a 2nd operator with similar profiles and scale the model.

### Phase 3: Ecosystem Orchestration (Weeks 24+)
1. **Once you have 2–3 case studies**, approach OEMs and print providers with demand-side leverage.
2. **Build the platform** (digital TDP registry, print-job orchestration, royalty settlement) *after* you've proven the business case, not before.

## Critical Risks & Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| OEM IP resistance | Start with orphaned/legacy parts; build rev-share contracts; involve OEM legal early |
| Qualification delays (aerospace/defense) | Avoid mission-critical parts in Phase 1; document acceptance criteria; plan long-term regulatory path |
| Pilot operator balks at new process | Identify internal sponsor; quantify their ROI; offer to absorb initial training/validation costs |
| Print provider unreliability | Vet 2+ providers; contractual SLA on lead-time & quality; redundancy for critical parts |
| Narrow TAM in Canada | Expand pitch to US operators and government; position as "North American supply-chain resilience" |

---

## Final Thought: The Founder's Mindset Shift

The expert's closing advice: **"Don't give up on the big vision. But see if there's something you can make money on in the meantime… Start with a single-customer digital inventory for one company with one supplier that can print those things. Doesn't have to be a platform to start."**

This is the core insight: **your vision is the compass, but your immediate business is a narrow, repeatable, high-ROI use-case.** Once that's proven and funded, you expand. You don't fund the vision directly; you fund the repeatable wedge, and the vision emerges from scaling the wedge.

The founder who can hold both — ambition to build an ecosystem AND discipline to start with a single operator-supplier dyad — will win.
