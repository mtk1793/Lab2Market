## KEY INTERVIEWS & FINDINGS

### Interview #45: Adam Saxty — Lloyd's Register (UK)
**Date:** February 18, 2026  
**Position:** Lead AM Technologist, Technical Directorate  
**Rating:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ **EXTRAORDINARY — Technical Authority**

#### Breakthrough Insight: The 7-Step AM Certification Pathway

Adam provided the **most authoritative AM certification breakdown** in 48+ interviews. This defines the technical roadmap for the platform.

**The Complete Certification Process:**

1. **Design Review** — Part suitability, geometry, build orientation, support structures
2. **Material Specification** — No universal AM standards exist (MMPDS gap); designer must produce custom material spec per part
3. **Facility Qualification** — Personnel training, machine calibration, post-processing, CAD-to-machine translation (✅ **Done ONCE per facility**)
4. **Feedstock Qualification** — Powder reuse tracking, batch consistency, chemistry drift monitoring
5. **Part Qualification (Prototype)** — Build prototype, destructive testing, prove performance ≥ conventional equivalent
6. **Production Certification** — Build test coupons alongside parts, mechanical testing, functional testing (pressure, load)
7. **Installation Approval** — All documentation complete → part certified → installed

**Critical Commercial Insight:**
> "Once we qualify the part and we're ready for production... there might be different certification per order. Most 3D printing centers are kind of doing that with their builds already."

**Platform Implication:**
| Phase | Frequency | Platform Value |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Facility + feedstock qualification (Steps 3-4) | **ONCE** per facility | Atlantic XL pre-qualified |
| Design + part qualification (Steps 1-2, 5) | **ONCE** per part design | **Build into pre-certified parts library** |
| Production + test coupon (Steps 6-7) | **Every order** | Lightweight per-order verification |

**This validates the pre-certified parts library as technically sound and commercially defensible.**

#### Machine Variability Challenge

> "My number one concern — many of you're printing a replacement part, it's got to perform as good as the part it's replacing, if not better. And how are we going to prove that out?"

- Different AM machines produce different results even with identical parameters
- On-site printing (e.g., shipboard AM) introduces massive variability
- Solution: **Centralized certified facilities** (like Atlantic XL) with validated machines

### Interview: Craig Dicken — ExxonMobil
**Date:** March 3, 2026  
**Position:** Materials Management Senior Principal, Global Supply Chain  
**Background:** 20+ years MRO supply chain, formerly Canada Supply Chain Manager at Imperial Oil  
**Rating:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ **HIGH STRATEGIC VALUE**

#### Breakthrough #1: Global Visibility is Broken

> "We could stock the same part right? Multiple regions across the world and not actually know what other organizations have it. When we get into a pinch... we could potentially get it from another location. We don't have good lineup sight to that today."

**Implication:** Even $400B+ companies like ExxonMobil lack cross-region parts visibility. Multiple $10k–$100k+ parts warehoused redundantly across 50+ facilities with zero visibility.

**Platform Value:** Cross-facility parts sharing + on-demand fulfillment, not just visibility dashboards.

#### Breakthrough #2: Manual Intervention is Massive

> "There's a significant amount of manual intervention today because we are spread across multiple systems. Whenever a systematic process doesn't work, it requires human intervention... all the way through to the transmission of POs or other types of orders."

**Systems-of-Systems Integration Gap:**
- ExxonMobil runs: SAP (financials) + TM (transportation) + EWM (warehousing) + external vendor systems
- None talk seamlessly
- When POs don't reach vendors or updates don't reach end-users, supply chain breaks

**Platform Positioning:** Not "Buy our digital inventory software"—it's **"We reduce manual handoffs between your SAP, vendor systems, and execution layer."**

#### Breakthrough #3: DRL 3 Is the Real Barrier (CRITICAL)
#### Breakthrough #3: DRL 3 Is the Real Barrier (CRITICAL)

> **Craig's Direct Quote:** "How do you scale that digital readiness in a way that you do not have to spend a disproportionate amount of money getting that image ready? Getting to DRL 3 is not easy because of all the other things that need to be layered on more than just the scan. But the heat treatment and all the other quality pieces that are required to make a part actually ready to be manufactured."

**Digital Readiness Levels (DRL):**
- **DRL 1:** Initial concept
- **DRL 2:** Design + basic validation  
- **DRL 3:** Full manufacturing-ready design + materials data + heat treatment + QA protocols + **certification**

**Current Problem:**
- Each part requires custom engineering work to reach DRL 3
- Cost: $10k–$50k per part
- Timeline: 3–6 months
- This blocks enterprise AM adoption at scale

**Platform Solution:**
- Create **pre-certified parts library** that amortizes DRL 3 cost across 100+ operators
- Lloyd's certifies once → reusable globally across all customers
- Instead of one operator spending $50k, 10 operators share cost (platform takes 20–30%, they share rest)
- **This is the defensible moat**

#### MRO Supply Chains Are Reactive, Not Predictive

> "In the MRO supply chain, we are not as sophisticated... it's almost like a last-minute supply chain, which creates a lot of pressure because we either don't have the right materials in the warehouse, the lead time in the system is not accurate enough."

**Even ExxonMobil runs reactive MRO.** Lead-time data is wrong. Operators order "direct" without reliable visibility.

**Platform Positioning:** "From Reactive to Predictive MRO" — integration with classification societies (Lloyd's, DNV) + AM facilities gives operators the lead-time confidence they're missing.

### Interview: Douglas Garcia-Golindano — Equinor
**Date:** February 18, 2026  
**Position:** Midstream Asset Manager  
**Location:** Houston, Texas (working Argentina operations)  
**Background:** 26 years oil & gas across upstream, downstream, midstream  
**Rating:** ⭐⭐⭐ **VALUABLE OPERATOR ECONOMICS**

#### The Cascading Shutdown Economics — 1–2% Capex Protects 98% of Value

> **Douglas:** "We do what we call capital maintenance, which is critical spare parts that need to be maintained at all time. Why we consider this important is that upstream will not happen if midstream has a situation."

**The Cascade Effect:**
1. Midstream pipeline shutoff valve fails → must shut down pipeline
2. If pipeline shuts → upstream must stop production  
3. Shutting a well → well often damaged → costs millions to re-drill → months to recover
4. Refinery downstream hydrocracker fails → refinery shuts → storage fills → upstream must shut

> "My cost is gonna be very minimal compared to what I'm causing to the whole [system]... comparing midstream to the total capex — it's 1 to 2%. If you don't have it, you can lose 98% of your production."

**Critical Data Points:**
- Critical parts: 10–18 month lead times
- Spare parts inventory: 1–2% of capex  
- Production value protected: 98%
- **Bay du Nord:** $14B project targeting 2030–2033 first oil (conditions harsher than Norway)

**Strategic Implication:** Spare parts are not "cost center"—they're **production insurance**. Small investment protects massive production value.

### Interview: Matthew Erning — GKN Aerospace (UK)
**Date:** February 18, 2026  
**Position:** Technical Authority — Direct Energy Deposition (DED)  
**Background:** Dalhousie BEng + PhD (Materials); Boeing Defense; Tronosjet PEI (FAA PMA certification); GKN UK  
**Rating:** ⭐⭐⭐ **HONEST SKEPTIC — Aerospace Reality Check**

#### Aerospace Certification ≠ Marine/Offshore Certification

**What Is Actually Certified in Aerospace:**
> "Typically, you don't have individual parts that are certified. Platforms are certified... for example, Boeing certifies the 737."

- **Certified:** Platforms (airframes, engines)—by FAA, EASA, TCCA
- **NOT certified individually:** Parts, brackets, components
- GKN (Tier-1 supplier) **supports** OEM certification; doesn't certify themselves

**AM Is NOT Treated Differently by Regulators:**
> "The regulators by FAA and EASA have made it very clear that the regulations are process and material agnostic largely."

**The Real Aerospace Barrier: MMPDS Gap**
> "The problem with additive right now is those [material databases] don't exist. Where traditionally... you go to the MMPDS, which is essentially the Bible of material performance for aerospace. You pull out Ti-64 data. It doesn't exist for additive manufacturing."

**Strategic Implication:**
- **Marine/Offshore:** Regulatory framework not yet developed for AM (Adam Saxty confirmed)  
- **Aerospace:** Regulations exist and are AM-agnostic, but **material data doesn't exist** (MMPDS gap)
- **Do not conflate aerospace and marine/offshore certification in pitch**

#### Digital Inventory NOT Relevant to Civilian Aviation Production

> "From a civilian aviation ongoing production point of view, this digital inventory and on-demand processing — it's not quite as important... the production we're looking at is very well-defined."

- Production rates known far in advance (A320 rate, 737 rate)
- Supply chain planned accordingly
- **No "just-in-case" inventory problem** in civilian aviation production

**Where It Applies:** Aerospace MRO (different buyer), Defense (unpredictable demand)
