## Part 1: The Big Picture

### What Is AddManuChain?

AddManuChain (operating under the startup name Alma-Tech) is a platform concept designed to replace physical spare parts inventory with certified digital files that can be manufactured on-demand via additive manufacturing (3D printing). At its core sits an AI Trust Layer that generates "Digital Birth Certificates" for quality assurance — linking design files, material specifications, process parameters, and certification records into a single verifiable digital asset.

The platform connects three parties: operators who need parts, AM facilities who can print them, and classification societies who certify the output. The vision is to eliminate costly warehousing, reduce emergency logistics (helicopters, charter flights), and cut downtime on remote industrial assets — offshore platforms, ships, Arctic mines, and military vessels.

### The Core Problem: The "War on Inventory"

The phrase "War on Inventory" — validated by Tyler Beatty at ExxonMobil as actual internal corporate terminology — captures the central tension the platform addresses. Industrial operators face two competing costs that cannot both be minimized:

The first is **visible inventory cost**: warehouse space, insurance, preservation, human capital, and tied-up working capital. Jason Power (Noble Corporation / Valaris) quantified this at $30–50M in physical spare parts per offshore drilling vessel. Kwadwo Ampofo (Deloitte) framed it as a CFO-level problem — more spare parts means increased working capital with zero productive return.

The second is **invisible emergency cost**: when a part is needed and unavailable, operators resort to "hot-shotting" — chartering aircraft, helicopters, or cougar flights for single components. Tyler Beatty referenced chartering an aircraft for a doorknob. Adam Mars (Rosen Maritime) quantified daily downtime at $50K–$100K per day for maritime vessels. For offshore platforms, the figure runs far higher — Mike Paulin (C-CORE) referenced production rates of up to 100,000 barrels/day multiplied by the oil price.

Douglas Garcia-Golindano (Equinor) provided the most powerful framing of this tension: spare parts represent only 1–2% of total project capex, but they protect 98% of production value. A single valve failure in midstream can cascade upstream (forcing well shutdown, potentially damaging the well and requiring millions to re-drill) and downstream (filling storage, shutting refineries). Critical parts have 10–18 month lead times.

Budget politics make the problem worse. Tyler Beatty explained that inventory is a visible line item that gets cut in annual budget reviews, while hot-shotting costs are accepted because they're tied to urgency. Companies systematically under-invest in the thing that protects them.

## Part 3: What Was Validated

### Hypothesis 1: The Spare Parts Pain Is Real — STRONGLY VALIDATED

Every single interview with an operator, service provider, or logistics professional confirmed that spare parts management is a major, ongoing pain. The evidence is overwhelming:

- ExxonMobil uses the internal term "War on Inventory" to describe the tension (Tyler Beatty)
- $30–50M per vessel in physical inventory (Jason Power, Noble/Valaris)
- $4–5M inventory for $50M asset base in Arctic utilities (Adam Chubbs)
- $50K–$100K/day vessel downtime (Adam Mars, Rosen Maritime)
- Up to $7M/day for offshore platform downtime (production rate × oil price) (Mike Paulin)
- 10–18 month lead times on critical parts (Douglas Garcia-Golindano, Equinor)
- 10-day emergency delivery for a motor in Central Newfoundland (Darren Wood)
- Chartering aircraft for single components is routine (Tyler Beatty)
- N+1 or N+2 redundancy required in remote operations (Adam Chubbs)
- 1–2% of capex in spare parts protects 98% of production value (Douglas, Equinor)

### Hypothesis 2: Certification Is THE Barrier — STRONGLY VALIDATED

Certification emerged as the single most cited barrier to AM adoption across all sectors:

- "Certifying authorities haven't led the charge" (Tyler Beatty, ExxonMobil)
- "AM developed so fast that authorities couldn't catch up" (Dr. Ali Nasiri)
- "There's just lives at stake, it's super scary for someone to agree to that" (Aaron Plamondon, Irving)
- "I don't think there IS a certification level" for Navy AM parts (Aaron Plamondon)
- No universal AM material standards exist yet — designer must produce custom spec per part (Adam Saxty, Lloyd's Register)
- 2+ year minimum certification timeline through DNV, ABS, or Lloyd's (Jim Granger, MAN Energy Solutions)
- Only ~2 companies in Canada do shock/vibration testing for defence (Aaron Plamondon)

However, the barrier is sector-dependent. For plastic prototyping and consumer products (Agile Manufacturing's world), finance is the barrier. For marine, defence, and oil & gas, certification is the barrier.

### Hypothesis 3: Digital Inventory Is Technically Feasible — VALIDATED

Multiple interviews confirmed the technical feasibility of digital inventory:

- ERP integration is feasible — SAP's MRP engine can handle digital inventory (Kwadwo Ampofo, Deloitte)
- MAN Energy Solutions already uses digital inventory extensively (Jim Granger)
- Equinor and ExxonMobil already implementing digital inventory via Siemens (Dr. Ali Nasiri)
- OEMs prefer connecting to local AM facilities over shipping from Europe/Norway (Dr. Ali Nasiri)
- Facility and part qualification are one-time investments, not recurring costs (Trevor Butler / Adam Saxty, Lloyd's Register)

### Hypothesis 4: IP Security Is a Top Barrier — STRONGLY VALIDATED

IP protection was raised independently in nearly every interview:

- Companies unwilling to share IP regardless of NDA strength (Jim Granger, MAN)
- IP security is #1 barrier — more critical than cost or time (Nichelle, Assembly Studio)
- Controlled goods access in aerospace/defence (Arad Gharagozli, Galaxia)
- OEM IP and liability ownership are the two biggest structural barriers (Aker Solutions)
- "The IP is gonna be huge — security, making sure your stuff isn't gonna get hacked" (Sheila Paterson)

### Hypothesis 5: Data Quality Blocks AI Adoption — VALIDATED

Multiple sources confirmed that data quality, not AI capability, is the bottleneck:

- "AI is only as good as the collection of the data. And that part is just a little too unwieldy" (Aaron Plamondon, Irving)
- "The data is massive. Some of it's not digital. Some of it's pen and paper, spreadsheets" (Aaron Plamondon)
- "In the MRO supply chain, we are not as sophisticated" — lead-time data is inaccurate (Craig Dicken, ExxonMobil)
- Data quality across the multi-party shipping ecosystem is the real blocker (André Simha, MSC)
- "I think you're going to have a really hard sell with selling AI" (Shawn Sooley, Shield Group)

### Hypothesis 6: Technology Adoption Is Political, Not Technical — VALIDATED

The barrier to platform adoption is organizational inertia and political risk, not technology:

- "Most liked to work traditional — they don't like modern ways of working" (Ali, Interview #47)
- "Until the Navy says 'go do this,' we're not going to get anywhere" (Aaron Plamondon)
- "They don't know that it's a problem that can be solved" — operators have normalized the pain (Shawn Sooley)
- "Most people see things like additive manufacturing as a toy solution, not an industrial solution" (Shawn Sooley)
- TradeLens failed primarily because of trust, not technology (André Simha, MSC)
- Adoption triggers: endorsement from a major company, top-down mandate, or internal R&D legitimacy (Ali)

## Part 4: The Competitive Landscape

### FieldNode — Primary Direct Competitor

FieldNode (Norway, spun off from Fieldmade AS in 2020) is a SaaS platform for digital spare parts inventory with on-demand AM production orchestration. Their Industry Collaboration Project (ICP) includes ConocoPhillips, Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies, and ExxonMobil — all joined in January 2023. FieldNode was explicitly named as a competitor by Trevor Butler (Lloyd's Register) and confirmed as the platform Aker Solutions clients use.

FieldNode's strengths: established consortium of major O&G players, first-mover in the digital inventory space, proven track record in Norway. FieldNode's weaknesses: enterprise-focused (likely expensive), Norway-centric, no known Canadian presence.

### Pelagus 3D — Maritime Proof Point

Joint venture between Wilhelmsen and Thyssenkrupp. Focused on maritime digital spare parts. Validates the on-demand digital inventory model is operationally viable in the maritime sector.

### Shell's Digital Inventory Ecosystem

Shell has been developing internal digital inventory capabilities. Represents a proof point that major operators see this as strategic, not experimental.

### Atlantic XL — Local AM Facility (Potential Partner)

Just received Lloyd's Register's facility qualification certificate for AM — the first in the Americas. Both a potential competitor and potential partner for the platform.

### The Assembly Studio (PEI)

Complementary player focused on general manufacturing digital inventory with emphasis on IP security. Strong partnership potential rather than direct competition.

### Spare Parts 3D

Referenced by Nathan Smeenk as already delivering parts within 24 hours through existing networks.

### Key Competitive Insight

The market is validated but consolidating. AddManuChain's differentiation must come from either the AI Trust Layer / Digital Birth Certificate (quality assurance and certification integration), Canadian market focus (no equivalent presence exists), or integration across the full ecosystem (operators + AM facilities + classification societies + ERP systems).

## Part 5: Key Strategic Frameworks

### The Value–Capability–Permission Venn Diagram (Tyler Beatty)

Tyler Beatty contributed a framework that crystallizes the adoption challenge:

- **Value**: Industry recognizes the value of AM for spare parts — validated by every operator interview
- **Capability**: Technical capability exists — metals AM, polymer AM, DED, LPBF all proven
- **Permission**: Regulatory certification has not been granted at scale — classification societies haven't led the charge

All three circles must overlap for adoption. Currently, value and capability overlap, but permission remains the gap. AddManuChain's primary strategic role is to close the permission gap.

### The 7-Step AM Certification Pathway (Adam Saxty, Lloyd's Register)

The authoritative certification framework: (1) Design Review, (2) Material Specification, (3) Facility Qualification, (4) Feedstock Qualification, (5) Part Qualification, (6) Production Certification, (7) Installation Approval. Steps 1–5 are one-time per part/facility. Steps 6–7 repeat per order but are lightweight. This is the commercial case for a pre-certified parts library.

### The Aerospace First Strategy (Shawn Sooley)

Achieve aerospace certification first (the hardest standard), and every other sector (oil & gas, maritime, defence) becomes easy by comparison. This creates a credibility cascade.

### The Beachhead Sequence

Based on certification barrier analysis across all interviews:

1. **Arctic utilities / remote power** (lowest barrier — self-certification possible, CSA not always required)
2. **Maritime vessels** (DNV monitors equipment-level, not part-level; non-OEM parts already accepted)
3. **Offshore oil & gas** (FieldNode/Equinor already piloting; Bay du Nord is the greenfield opportunity)
4. **Naval defence** (requires admiral's decision + political mandate; Irving and Thales are gatekeepers)
5. **Aerospace** (product certification, not part certification; longest timeline but highest credibility payoff)

## Part 6: Recurring Themes Across All Interviews

### Theme 1: Obsolescence Is the Low-Hanging Fruit

Parts from 15–20-year-old install bases that are no longer manufactured by the original OEM represent the easiest entry point. No IP conflict (OEM has abandoned the product), clear pain (no alternative source), and lower certification complexity (replacing a part that no longer exists). Referenced by Aker Solutions, Darren Wood (Proax), Sean Morgan (Atlantic XL), and David Haire (CME NL).

### Theme 2: OEMs May Be Willing Partners, Not Adversaries

Sheila Paterson offered a critical reframe: many OEMs see spare parts as a non-core service burden. They may welcome a platform that serves their customers on their behalf while maintaining a revenue share. Dr. Ali Nasiri confirmed operators "prefer to be connected to local facilities to not always ship parts from Europe or Norway." The sales motion shifts from convincing resistant OEMs to offering them a licensing model that removes a burden.

### Theme 3: Start Non-Critical, Earn Trust, Escalate

Nearly every interviewee endorsed a phased approach: begin with non-critical, non-structural components (gaskets, housings, brackets, covers), demonstrate quality and reliability through the Digital Birth Certificate, then escalate to more critical applications. Clearwater Seafoods is already at this stage with plastic non-critical parts.

### Theme 4: Communication and Transparency Are Core Value

Bassey Amba's cross-segment analysis revealed communication as the universal pain point across all five customer segments. Craig Dicken confirmed "a significant amount of manual intervention today because we are spread across multiple systems." The platform's value may be less about the technology and more about orchestrating information flow across fragmented systems and stakeholders.

### Theme 5: Canada Is Behind, but That's the Opportunity

"Europe is 10+ years ahead" (Nichelle, Assembly Studio). Canadian companies are "timid and underfinanced" (Nathan Smeenk, Agile Manufacturing). But this means the Canadian market has unmet demand and no established platform presence. Atlantic XL's Lloyd's Register certification is a turning point for Canadian AM infrastructure.

### Theme 6: Trust and Governance Determine Ecosystem Success

André Simha's TradeLens lesson is definitive: if the platform is perceived as owned or controlled by one party, others will not participate. AddManuChain's governance model must be neutral — potentially a co-operative or multi-stakeholder structure. Data segregation is non-negotiable.

### Theme 7: The ROI Must Be 10–15% Minimum

Both Ali (Interview #47) and OMV Austria confirmed a hard 10–15% ROI threshold before any company will seriously evaluate a new technology platform. The ROI story must be built around: downtime reduction ($50K–$100K/day avoided), working capital freed (30% of $30–50M inventory), inventory holding cost saved (20–25%/year on stored value), and logistics cost eliminated (helicopter charters, expedited freight).

## Part 7: What Was Challenged or Rejected

### "I'm Not Convinced That Additive Enables Digital Inventory" — Mark Kirby

The most direct challenge to the core thesis. Kirby, with 13 years in metal AM, argued that digital inventory is not necessarily enabled by AM and that the business case (not certification) is the primary adoption barrier. His recommendation: position as "AI-powered supply chain decision support" with AM as one tool among many.

### "They've Got Their Shit Sorted Out" — Andrew Smith

Offshore NL rigs already share critical spares within 100km through equipment sharing agreements and helicopter transfers. The informal system works. AddManuChain's value must exceed what helicopter sharing already provides.

### "I Think You're Going to Have a Really Hard Sell with Selling AI" — Shawn Sooley

Operators are skeptical of AI pitches. They want proven solutions, not algorithms. The AI component should be invisible — powering decisions behind the scenes, not leading the marketing message.

### "Big Companies Don't Care About $500K–$1M/Day Downtime" — Nichelle, Assembly Studio

Large oil & gas majors have deep pockets and absorb downtime costs without urgency. The pain that motivates action is more often obsolescence (can't get the part at all) than cost (downtime is expensive but manageable).

### Space Constraints on Vessels — Clearwater Seafoods

Tony Jabbour raised a physical barrier no other interviewee mentioned: vessel space is fiercely prioritized, and an AM printer competes with revenue-generating equipment for deck or hold space.

### Aerospace Certification Is Fundamentally Different — GKN Aerospace

Matthew Erning corrected a key assumption: in civilian aerospace, products (entire aircraft systems) are certified, not individual parts. Digital inventory and on-demand AM are largely irrelevant to ongoing production. The defence angle is more promising.
