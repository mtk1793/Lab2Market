## The Core Problem (Validated Across 65+ Interviews)

The conventional narrative says: *"Remote industries need on-site 3D printers to eliminate long supply chain delays."*

The **real problem** (validated through 65+ interviews with operators) is different: **Operators don't have global visibility into where parts actually exist, manual handoffs break supply chain processes, and even when AM could work, the cost of certifying each part is prohibitive.**

**Here's the evidence from enterprise customers:**

**1. "Just-in-Case" Economics Are a Billion-Dollar Problem**
- Equinor: 80% of their $4.5B physical inventory sits unused (parts stockpiled "just in case")
- GE Oil & Gas: up to $88M in *annual downtime costs* from supply chain failures
- ExxonMobil (Craig Dicken, Materials Management Senior Principal): *"We could stock the same part in 5 global regions and not know we have it."*

**2. The Real Bottleneck: Manual Handoffs & Lost Visibility**
- Craig Dicken: *"There's a significant amount of manual intervention. When systems don't work, it requires human intervention... POs don't reach vendors, creating supply chain disruption."*
- Current reality: SAP systems, TMS systems, warehouse systems, vendor portals — none talk seamlessly
- Result: Emergency procurement instead of intelligent routing; redundant ordering; wasted inventory

**3. The Hidden Barrier to AM Adoption: Digital Readiness Scaling**
- The breakthrough insight from Craig Dicken (ExxonMobil, 20+ years MRO supply chain): 
> *"Just because a part can be 3D-printed doesn't mean it should be. Getting to Digital Readiness Level 3 [manufacturing-ready] is not easy — you need more than just CAD files. You need heat treatment specs, quality assurance protocols, materials data. How do you scale that without spending a disproportionate amount of money getting each design ready?"*

**This is the core insight:** The barrier to AM adoption is NOT "Can we print it?" — it's **"Can we certify it affordably and reuse that certification across multiple operators?"**

## Why NRC-IRAP Funding Fits Perfectly: Solving the DRL 3 Scaling Problem

**The Core Barrier (From ExxonMobil Interview):**
The real blocker to AM adoption is **not** "Can we 3D print?" (we can). It's **"Can we certify parts affordably and scale certification across multiple operators?"**

ExxonMobil's Materials Management organization spends $10k–$50k to certify *each* part for additive manufacturing. At scale, this becomes prohibitively expensive. Our breakthrough insight: **create a pre-certified parts library where one certification serves 100+ operators** — amortize the certification cost across a network.

**This is where NRC-IRAP R&D support becomes critical:**

### **R&D Gap #1: Materials Data Standardization** (6–12 months)
- **Problem:** Each Classification Society (Lloyd's, DNV, ABS) manages materials data differently. Operators can't reuse certified data across facilities or regions.
- **IRAP R&D:** Co-develop a unified materials data layer with Lloyd's Register that maps aerospace specs (MMPDS), O&G specs (DNV standards), maritime specs into a standardized digital format
- **Outcome:** Design Part A is certified in Facility 1 (materials = Ti-6Al-4V, hardness X, fatigue Y); that certification becomes reusable in Facility 2 without re-engineering
- **Impact:** Reduces per-part certification cost from $10k–$50k to $2k–$5k (80% cost reduction)

### **R&D Gap #2: Design-for-AM Decision Engine** (3–6 months)
- **Problem:** Determining "which parts should be AM" requires manual engineering assessment by trained specialists
- **IRAP R&D:** Build an AI/ML-assisted decision engine that encodes industry standards (military spec MIL-A-8591, DNV Standard for Classification Societies, ABS Manufacturing Directives)
- **Rules:** Cost threshold ($5k savings minimum), lead-time threshold (8+ week OEM), criticality (non-safety-critical or dual-certified), geometry suitability
- **Outcome:** Automated recommendation system; reduces assessment time from 2–4 weeks to 2–4 days
- **Impact:** Enables Equinor or Shell to evaluate 1,000+ parts in their inventory; identify 50–100 AM-suitable candidates automatically

### **R&D Gap #3: Multi-Party Design Security & Escrow** (3–6 months)
- **Problem:** OEMs won't share designs unless IP is protected; operators won't adopt unless data is segregated and confidential
- **IRAP R&D:** Build encrypted design vault (AES-256), blockchain audit log, multi-party access controls
- **Implementation:** Design Part A (owned by OEM 1) locked to Operator 1; Operator 2 cannot view; certification metadata shared without exposing designs
- **Outcome:** Proof of trust; enables OEMs to participate in the ecosystem without IP risk
- **Impact:** Critical for enterprise adoption; currently our biggest adoption blocker

### **R&D Gap #4: Classification Society Workflow Integration** (3–9 months)
- **Problem:** Certification pathways differ by society. No unified process exists; manual coordination required
- **IRAP R&D:** Build API connectors and standardized workflows for Lloyd's Type Approval, DNV Risk Assessment, ABS Design Approval
- **Implementation:** Platform becomes the coordination hub; technical drawings → assessment → approval → certification without manual email/spreadsheet coordination
- **Outcome:** Reduces 12-week certification path to 4–6 weeks; enables weekly certification batches (vs. ad-hoc)
- **Impact:** Amortization economics work only at scale; this R&D unlocks scale
