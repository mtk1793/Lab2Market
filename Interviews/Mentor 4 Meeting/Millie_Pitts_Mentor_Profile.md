## Harvard Business School Study: What Makes Startups Fail

### Failure Pattern 1: Domain Expertise Gap
**Problem:** Founders without expertise in their target domain

Examples of failure:
- "I want to be an entrepreneur" → decides to build ocean autonomous vehicle
- No engineering background
- No ocean domain knowledge
- Fatal lack of technical credibility

**Parallel Domain (Jens Kroeger context):** Similar to restaurant failures - domain expertise critical

### Failure Pattern 2: Founder Over-Specialization Without Complementary Co-Founders
**Problem:** Narrow expertise without complementary skills

- Founder expert in one discipline (e.g., materials science)
- Weak in other critical areas (business, manufacturing, markets)
- **Solution:** Complementary co-founder with different domain expertise
- **Success factor:** Right team composition from day one

### Reverse Engineering Success
**What WORKS:**
- All right domain expertise represented on founding team
- Complementary skill sets
- Founder cohesion and shared vision

---

## Failure Pattern 3: Premature Iteration Without Customer Discovery

### The "Tinker Problem"
**Pattern observed repeatedly:**
1. Founder gets initial funding (grants, friends/family)
2. Gets excited and starts tinkering with product
3. Talks to one customer → customer wants modification
4. Tinkers product → asks next customer
5. Next customer wants something different
6. Repeat cycle...

### Consequence
- **Burn through capital** developing custom variations
- **Never build viable product** that can go to market
- **Run out of money** before achieving repeatability
- **Try to raise more capital** with no real product
- **Investors ask:** "What happened to the money we already gave you?"

### The Real Problem
- **Lack of robust customer discovery**
- Didn't understand full market before iterating
- Confused "customer feedback" with "market demand"
- Built custom solutions instead of scalable product

## Critical Success Factors for Ocean Tech (& Beyond)

### 1. Customer Discovery ≠ Product Iteration
- **Must precede** building and modifying product
- Survey broad market segment
- Understand common needs vs. edge cases
- Identify your specific niche (intersection of ability + market demand)

### 2. Complementarity in Founding Team
- Different domain expertise
- Different soft skills
- Ability to challenge each other constructively
- Covering all critical success factors

### 3. Founder Mindset: Know Yourself
- **Not everyone is cut out for entrepreneurship**
- Some excellent scientists/engineers should NOT start companies
- Example: "I never want to be responsible for payroll" → that person shouldn't start company
- **Valid path alternative:** Contribute innovation within existing company structure

### 4. Business Model Clarity
- Product vs. Service distinction matters
- Revenue model sustainability
- Market size addressable with chosen model

## Millie's Direct Advice: Do You WANT to Be an Entrepreneur?

### Self-Assessment Framework
**Questions founders should ask honestly:**

1. **Risk tolerance:** Can you handle losing it all?
2. **Payroll responsibility:** Can you handle being responsible for employees' mortgages?
3. **Time horizon:** Are you willing to wait 5-10-15 years for success?
4. **Personal situation:**
   - Do you have family dependents?
   - Other sources of income available?
   - Financial runway?
5. **Alternative paths:** Does this have to be via startup or could you:
   - Work in R&D within large company?
   - Be technical co-founder without CEO role?
   - Be advisor/board member?

### Key Realization
"Not everyone who has innovation has to start a startup. There are other mechanisms."

**Valid alternative paths:**
- Technical advisor role
- Equity holder without management responsibility
- CTO/Chief Scientist role within larger company
- IP licensing/royalties
- Consulting
