# Interview Questions & Answers: Bob Hedrick

## Q1 — Software & Manufacturing Ecosystem Evolution

**Q:** Across the manufacturing software industry, how have vendor relationships and product ecosystems evolved? What's the biggest challenge manufacturers face adopting new tools alongside existing systems?

**A (from transcript):** Bob specializes in a niche-within-a-niche: directed energy deposition (DED) and hybrid additive-subtractive systems. His company is built on top of the Mastercam development environment — inheriting its ecosystem credibility without needing to build from scratch. The biggest barrier he identifies is **software immaturity** combined with **expertise gap**: the body of institutional knowledge needed to program DED systems simply doesn't exist broadly yet. Traditional machining has 70+ years of codified practice; DED has ~15 years. Competitors are Siemens and Autodesk — with whom he cannot compete on scale, so he survives by specializing where they don't go.

**Key Quote:** *"The body of knowledge you need to programme these systems — you need a lot of experience. The institutional knowledge isn't there for this technology."*

## Q2 — The Research-to-Product Gap

**Q:** What are the biggest reasons promising ideas never make it to market? How do you distinguish genuine market demand from interesting-but-niche?

**A (from transcript):** Bob describes a systemic failure in the Canadian research commercialization pipeline. His wife (university professor, co-founder) is blocked from using their own Canadian company as an "industrial partner" — classified as a conflict of interest. Universities only recognize large foreign companies (Autodesk, Siemens) as qualifying partners. The result: Canadian IP developed at Canadian universities is systematically redirected to foreign companies. He also described the NRC telling him to "wait for big American companies to do it" — institutional passivity that kills domestic innovation.

**Key Quote:** *"They said conflict of interest. How is a Canadian company doing well because of research in Canada — how is that a bad thing?"*

**Key Quote:** *"The NRC asked me: 'Why are you doing this? Wait for the big American companies to develop it.' I was banging my head."*

## Q3 — AI and Machine Learning in Manufacturing Software

**Q (evolved from transcript):** Are you using AI or ML in your software? Do you think AI has a place in manufacturing tooling?

**A (from transcript):** Bob uses AI specifically for thermal compensation — one of the most computationally expensive problems in DED: predicting residual stress and distortion during and after metal deposition. Traditional finite element analysis (FEA via ANSYS) takes a week to run. His neural network model runs the equivalent in seconds. He is dismissive of AI applied vaguely and emphasizes that the application must solve a specific, measurable bottleneck. He is also working with ANSYS to integrate his AI-accelerated model into their environment.

**Key Quote:** *"ANSYS runs a thermal model for a week. We do it in seconds."*

**Key Quote:** *"Applying AI to 3D geometries is really hard. You need to find the specific application — then it's extremely powerful."*

## Q4 — Defense / Navy Use of Additive Manufacturing

**Q (evolved from transcript):** Have you pursued the Canadian defense market? How do you see AM fitting into naval and defense supply chains?

**A (from transcript):** Bob attended a DND meeting in Toronto and met privately with a representative. He proposed integrating additive manufacturing (through OEM machine partnerships) into military supply chains — citing containerized "machine shop in a box" systems already being used by the US Navy. The DND contact insisted on a Canadian machine tool builder as co-applicant — a requirement no existing Canadian company can meet in this niche. He was effectively shut out. His conclusion: Canada's defense procurement policy, designed for mature industries, is a structural veto for frontier technology innovators.

**US Navy Validation:** Bob confirmed the USS Bataan deployment of onboard printing. Primary benefit: if a part breaks, they manufacture it on-site — eliminating 6-month military supply chain waits. He also noted containerized hybrid machines that can be deployed to any location.

**Key Quote:** *"The advantage is: if they break a part, they can make it right there. They don't have to go through the military supply chain — which might take 6 months."*

## Q5 — Market Viability and Platform Strategy

**Q:** Is there a market for a platform connecting AM facilities, OEMs, and end users? Dead end or opportunity?

**A (from transcript):** Bob's response is honest and nuanced. He agrees the concept is valid but warns about pace of adoption. His own company entered the DED software market before Siemens, and 14 years later adoption is still immature. He explicitly advises against a Canada-only strategy (near-zero Canadian customers for him despite being based there). He validates the digital inventory concept independently — identifying automotive spare parts (10-year OEM inventory requirements) as an analogy where AM clearly makes sense. He emphasizes that the urgency to invest in AM printing comes only AFTER a company already has a machine — meaning the platform must either bundle hardware adoption or target companies already equipped.

**Key Quote:** *"Why keep 10 years of auto spare parts in a warehouse? You could 3D print it — if it functions the same and it's non-visible, why would you care?"*

**Key Quote:** *"In the Canadian market, I definitely wouldn't develop anything for additive. There are less than a dozen companies using the technology we support."*

## Bonus Q1 — OEM Relationships

**Q:** As a software platform, how do you deal with OEMs? How do you convince them to work with you?

**A (from transcript):** Bob never cold-pitches. Most OEMs came to him. His approach: offer a 90-day trial (low commitment), then bring his wife and PhD students to demonstrate research depth — thermal modeling, material science, wear characterization. He shows OEMs that his company is a long-term research partner, not just a software vendor. He notes that being built on top of Mastercam (a trusted brand) gives him credibility.

**Key Quote:** *"Most of them came to us. We haven't done a lot of advertising. I bring in my wife and PhD students and say — here's where we are, here's the research we're doing."*

---

## Bonus Q2 — Non-Critical Parts as Entry Point

**Q:** What is the right starting point for AM in industrial supply chains?

**A (from transcript):** Bob explicitly frames non-critical replacement parts as the correct AM market entry:  replacement parts for ships and industrial equipment — things that aren't going to cause catastrophic failure, but whose absence halts operations. He contrasts this to GE's approach of starting with jet engine nozzles (3 years to certify, highest possible risk). He also cited a Norwegian company (Norsk Titanium) working on aircraft structural components as an example of how the industry is gradually scaling toward critical parts — but only after proving the model on non-critical applications first.

**Key Quote:** *"Start with replacement parts for ships — things that aren't going to cause the ship to sink, but things that break and take forever to get. That's the right entry point."*

## Platform Reaction (End of Interview)

**Researcher's pitch:** A platform building an ecosystem between AM facilities, OEMs, regulation bodies, and end users — enabling digital inventory, on-site printing, and AI-automated sourcing for offshore/Arctic operators.

**Bob's reaction:** The biggest hurdle is convincing operators to invest in 3D printing hardware *before* they need the software/platform. He saw the same pattern with DND. He validated the offshore/Arctic angle strongly (wife's research involvement with remote northern communities receiving annual shipments). Suggested focusing on automotive spare parts as a parallel analogy. Encouraged pursuing global markets from day one rather than trying to build in Canada.

**Key Quote:** *"Have something global, not Canadian. I'm sure you'll have to do that."*
