# Interview 58: Deep Strategic Insights — Manufacturing Software, Ecosystem Strategy & the Hardware-First Bottleneck

**Interview Subject:** Bob Hedrick, President & Co-founder of CAMufacturing Solutions Inc.; specializes in directedenergy deposition (DED) and hybrid additive-subtractive manufacturing software; PhD-level thermal modeling, AI-accelerated simulation; 14+ years in AM software commercialization.

**Company Profile:** CAMufacturing is built on top of Mastercam (industry-standard CAM software), focusing on niche specialization (DED + hybrid systems) where larger vendors (Siemens, Autodesk) don't compete.

**Thesis:** Your platform faces a critical chicken-and-egg problem that Bob's 14-year journey reveals: **companies won't adopt AM software/platforms until they already have AM hardware**. Additionally, Canada is an unsuitable market for this venture; global expansion and hardware bundling are non-negotiable prerequisites.

---

## Core Strategic Tensions & Insights

### 1. **The Hardware-First Bottleneck: Why Software Platforms Fail Before They Scale**

Bob's experience reveals a counterintuitive but critical insight: **software adoption in manufacturing is hardware-gated.**

**The Pattern** (from his DED software history):
- CAMufacturing Solutions developed specialized DED programming software
- Entered a market where Siemens and Autodesk existed but didn't focus on DED
- 14+ years later, penetration is still immature; only "a few dozen" companies globally are using sophisticated DED systems
- His Canadian customer base: **less than a dozen companies** despite being in Canada

**Why This Matters for Your Platform:**
Your digital inventory + AM platform assumes companies have AM hardware (3D printers, metal deposition systems, etc.). But the reality is:
- Most manufacturers still don't own AM equipment
- Those who do own it often run it in test/prototype mode, not production
- Adoption of specialized manufacturing hardware follows a **decades-long S-curve**, not a rapid tech adoption curve

**The Implication:**
If you build a platform that requires companies to:
1. Buy or build a 3D printer ($200k–$2M+ for industrial systems)
2. Train operators (18–24 months to proficiency)
3. Qualify parts and processes (6–12 months)
4. Then use your software to manage part sourcing, printing, and certification (additional complexity)

...you're asking for a 3–4 year commitment before any ROI. Most companies won't do this.

**Your Strategy Must Solve This:**
- **Option A (Hardware Bundling)**: Partner with printer manufacturers to offer bundled hardware + software + training + support. This increases your addressable market and lowers friction.
- **Option B (Print Service Aggregation)**: Position your platform as software for companies using *someone else's printers*. You manage the ecosystem (finding printers, quality control, logistics); they use your software interface.
- **Option C (Hybrid Model)**: Offer both—hardware partnerships for willing adopters + software-only for companies with existing hardware.

Bob's advice: Start by identifying which segments already have hardware. Don't assume they need to buy it.

### 2. **The Canadian Dead End: Why a Canada-First Strategy Is Strategically Insolvent**

This is perhaps the most direct advice from Bob: **"In the Canadian market, I definitely wouldn't develop anything for additive. There are less than a dozen companies using the technology we support."**

**The Reality:**
- Bob is based in Canada, has built a world-class DED software solution, and yet has found minimal Canadian customers
- His company survives by serving US, European, and international customers
- The Canadian AM/advanced manufacturing market is simply too small

**Why Canada Underperforms in Advanced Manufacturing:**
1. **Small customer base**: Canada has ~4,000 primary manufacturers. Global R&D spending in AM is concentrated in US, Germany, Japan, South Korea
2. **Brain drain**: Canadian researchers and entrepreneurs migrate to where funding and customers are
3. **Capital constraints**: Smaller venture capital ecosystem means Canadian startups must grow slower or relocate
4. **End-user base**: Most Canadian manufacturers are SMEs, not large industrials that invest in frontier tech
5. **Regulatory isolation**: Canadian defense, aerospace, and maritime sectors are separate from larger North American markets; regulatory approvals don't transfer

**Strategic Implication:**
A "Platform for Canadian operators" is a non-viable business. Bob's evidence: He has invested 14+ years in a superior technology (Mastercam-based DED software with AI thermal modeling) and **still can't find a sustainable Canadian customer base**.

**What You Must Do Instead:**
- **Geographic expansion**: Design for North America (US + Canada) from day one. The US customer base is 10–20x larger than Canada
- **Global positioning**: If you can, immediately expand to Northern Europe, Asia-Pacific, and other regions where AM adoption is higher
- **Strategic focus**: Target sectors with high AM maturity globally (aerospace, defense, automotive OEM supply chains) where Canadian customers can participate as part of larger ecosystems

**The Specific Target**: Automotive aftermarket parts. Bob noted: "Why keep 10 years of auto spare parts in a warehouse? You could 3D print it." This analogy transfers to maritime, but global automotive is where the density exists.

### 3. **The Research-to-Product Gap: Institutional Barriers to Commercialization in Canada**

Bob shared a deeply frustrating experience that reveals structural problems in Canada's innovation ecosystem:

**The Problem** (his wife's experience as university co-founder):
- His wife is a university professor and co-founder of CAMufacturing
- Universities classify any company in which faculty have equity as a "conflict of interest"
- Result: she cannot use her own company (which she co-founded) as an "industrial partner" for university research projects
- Universities only recognize *large foreign companies* (Autodesk, Siemens) as acceptable industrial partners
- Outcome: Canadian university IP is systematically redirected to foreign companies

**The NRC Experience:**
- Bob approached the Canadian National Research Council (NRC) seeking support for his DED/AM initiative
- NRC's response: **"Why are you doing this? Wait for the big American companies to develop it."**
- Translation: Canadian institutions actively discourage domestic innovation in favor of American incumbents

**Why This Matters:**
Your platform will need partnerships with:
- Universities (for research validation, pilot testing, student recruitment)
- Government agencies (for funding, pilot programs, regulatory alignment)
- Research centers (NRC, IRAP, etc.)

If these institutions create barriers to Canadian companies (preferring foreign partners), **you face a structural headwind that product excellence cannot overcome.**

**Your Strategic Response:**
1. **Internationalize early**: Don't rely on Canadian institutional support. Build relationships with US and international research centers
2. **Pursue government funding strategically**: NRC-IRAP might be one avenue, but also pursue DND, DPA (defence, peace, security), US SBIR/STTR programs, and industry consortiums
3. **Form private-sector consortiums**: Bypass universities. Create industry partnerships (e.g., major shipbuilders, oil & gas operators) that fund R&D without institutional friction
4. **Consider international incorporation**: If Canadian institutions consistently prefer foreign companies, consider whether your company's legal domicile matters. Some founders domicile in US/UK while keeping R&D in Canada

Bob's frustration is justified: **You've built something better, but Canada's institutions actively prefer you to lose.** Don't let that be your company's bottleneck.

### 4. **Software Maturity vs. Institutional Knowledge: The 70-Year Gap Problem**

Bob identifies a critical issue: **The body of institutional knowledge needed to master a technology is proportional to how long it's existed.**

**The Comparison:**
- **Traditional CNC/CAM machining**: 70+ years of practice, codified knowledge, training programs, certification standards, expert communities
- **DED (Directed Energy Deposition)**: ~15 years of practical commercial use, scattered expertise, no standardized training, massive knowledge gap

**The Implication:**
When Bob's company enters the DED market, they're not just competing against Siemens; they're competing against a 50+ year gap in *how to teach, standardize, and operationalize* the technology.

**Why This Matters for Your Platform:**
Your AM platform faces the same knowledge gap:
- Part design for AM (vs. traditional manufacturing) is still evolving
- Qualification standards are emerging (ISO 23592) but not universally adopted
- Training to code parts for AM printing is sparse
- Troubleshooting and failure analysis lacks institutional precedent

**Your Must-Have Capability:**
Your platform needs to **encode institutional knowledge**:
1. **Design guidelines**: Teach users which parts are AM-suitable and which aren't
2. **Qualification standards**: Codify what DNV, ABS, and other bodies require for certification
3. **Training & certification**: Partner with schools, technical institutes, and industry groups to train operators
4. **Failure analysis & feedback loops**: Capture what fails, why, and share lessons across the network

If you position your platform as just "connecting printers to customers," you've missed the real value: **becoming the institutional knowledge repository for AM in industrial supply chains.**

### 5. **OEM Relationships: Trust Through Research Depth & Long-Term Commitment**

Bob's approach to OEM relationships offers a valuable blueprint:

**His Strategy:**
- Never cold-pitch
- Wait for OEMs to approach him (they do, because his thermal modeling is unique)
- When engaging, bring his wife (university professor), PhD students, and research artifacts
- Offer a 90-day trial (low commitment)
- Demonstrate research depth in material science, thermal compensation, wear characterization
- Position as a **long-term research partner**, not a software vendor
- Leverage Mastercam credibility (built on top of trusted ecosystem)

**Key Quote**: *"Most of them came to us. We haven't done a lot of advertising. I bring in my wife and PhD students and say—here's where we are, here's the research we're doing."*

**Why This Matters:**
OEMs (machinery manufacturers, pump companies, automotive tier-1s) are skeptical of startups. They've been burned before by software companies that promised integration but delivered nothing.

**What Convinces Them:**
- **Research credibility**: Can you demonstrate ongoing innovation?
- **Long-term viability**: Will you be around in 5 years?
- **Ecosystem fit**: Are you built on trusted platforms (like Mastercam)?
- **Low-risk entry**: Can they try you for 90 days without major commitment?

**How to Apply This to Your Platform:**
1. **Partner with established CAM/CAD vendors** (Mastercam, SolidWorks, Autodesk Fusion) so OEMs see you as ecosystem, not threat
2. **Publish research**: Database of part qualification studies, thermal analyses, failure case studies. Show institutional knowledge building
3. **University partnerships**: Position yourself as a research consortium, not just commercial software
4. **90-day pilots**: Offer low-friction trials with no long-term commitment
5. **OEM incentives**: Revenue-share models where OEMs benefit from increased part printing

### 6. **Defense Market Validation—And Why It's Blocked for Canadian Companies**

Bob pursued the Canadian Defense market and was shut out. His story reveals important lessons:

**What Bob Tried:**
- Attended DND meeting in Toronto
- Met privately with a representative
- Proposed integrating AM into military supply chains (citing successful US Navy onboard (containerized manufacturing systems)
- Pitched the value: reduce 6-month military supply chain waits to immediate onboard manufacturing

**What DND Said:**
*"You need a Canadian machine tool builder as co-applicant."*

**Why This Blocked Him:**
- No Canadian company manufactures industrial AM/hybrid systems at scale
- DND procurement policy, designed for mature industries, requires domestic manufacturing partnerships
- Frontier tech startups can't easily create manufacturing partnerships that don't exist

**But the US Navy Already Proved the Value:**
- USS Bataan and similar deployments use onboard AM systems
- Can manufacture critical replacement parts immediately
- Eliminates 6-month military-industrial supply chain dependency

**Strategic Implication for Your Platform:**
1. **Defense is a real market** (US Navy validation is strong)
2. **Canada's procurement structure blocks frontier innovators** (institutional bias toward established suppliers)
3. **However, you can pursue DND indirectly** — through larger defense contractors who have DND relationships (e.g., Aker Solutions, MDA, CAE)

**Your Strategy:**
- Don't pitch DND directly as a startup
- Position your platform as a capability for defense contractors to offer to DND
- Work through existing prime contractors who have procurement relationships
- Offer DND government-funded pilots through NRC-IRAP or DND Innovation programs (which have fewer supplier restrictions than direct procurement)

### 7. **The Entry Point: Non-Critical Replacement Parts**

Bob explicitly validates your entry strategy: **Start with non-critical replacement parts** (bearings, seals, housings for maritime equipment, industrial machinery, etc.) rather than trying to certify high-risk components.

**His Contrasts:**
- **GE's approach** (jet engine nozzles): Started with the most critical, highest-value components. Took 3+ years to certify. Massive regulatory burden upfront.
- **Norsk Titanium's approach** (aircraft structural components): Proved the model on less critical components first, then gradually scaled to structural parts.
- **Bob's recommendation** (*"Correct" entry point*): Replacement parts for ships — "things that aren't going to cause the ship to sink, but things that break and take forever to get."

**Why This Matters:**
- Regulatory bodies (DNV, ABS, FAA) have graduated approval pathways
- Non-critical parts require Type Approval (weeks to months)
- Structural/critical components require Design Approval (years)
- Start with Type Approval first; accumulate data and case studies; then tackle Design Approval

**Your Immediate Target:**
- Offshore equipment replacement parts (non-critical casings, brackets, ducting)
- Maritime non-critical spares (ship accommodation parts, non-structural fittings)
- Industrial equipment replacement parts (pump housings, bearing blocks)

These are high-volume, long-lead-time parts from OEMs where AM can deliver value immediately.

## Strategic Recommendations Based on Bob's Insights

### Phase 1: Hardware Bundling or Print-as-a-Service (Months 0–12)
1. **Option A**: Partner with 2–3 industrial AM manufacturers (GE Additive, Desktop Metal, Optomec) to offer bundled hardware + your software + training + support
2. **Option B**: Establish relationships with 15–20 existing print service providers (Matrical, 3D Systems, ExOne) to offer your software as the orchestration layer atop their equipment
3. **Option C**: Hybrid—do both with different customer segments

**Outcome**: Reduce the hardware adoption barrier. Don't ask customers to buy machines; let them use existing capacity or partner with you for bundled acquisition.

### Phase 2: Encode Institutional Knowledge (Months 0–ongoing)
1. **Build a design guidance system**: Which parts are AM-suitable? (Geometry, material, post-processing cost)
2. **Map regulatory pathways**: Type Approval (maritime), certification requirements by industry
3. **Create training curriculum**: Teach operators, designers, procurement teams how to use your platform effectively
4. **Publish case studies**: Document every pilot with quantified ROI, certification timeline, cost savings

**Outcome**: Position your platform as the knowledge repository, not just software. This increases stickiness and defensibility.

### Phase 3: OEM Partnerships (Months 6–18)
1. **Identify 5–10 target OEMs** (maritime equipment suppliers, pump manufacturers, aerospace tier-2s with highest part catalog density)
2. **Offer 90-day trials** with revenue-share licensing
3. **Bring research artifacts**: Partner with universities or your own R&D team to demonstrate ongoing innovation
4. **Position through ecosystem**: Build on Mastercam or SolidWorks, not as competitor

**Outcome**: OEM buy-in reduces certification friction and increases your credibility with end-users.

### Phase 4: Geographic Expansion (Months 12+)
1. **North America first** (US + Canada): Automotive aftermarket, marine, oil & gas
2. **Northern Europe second** (Baltic, North Sea): Higher AM adoption, strong digital infrastructure
3. **Global reach by Year 3**: Asia-Pacific, other regions

**Outcome**: Scale beyond Canada. Global market is 10–50x larger than Canadian market.

## Final Thought: Bob's 14-Year Journey Is Your Roadmap

Bob's experience with DED software encodes crucial lessons:
1. **Hardware adoption is slow**: 14 years in, penetration is still immature
2. **Niche specialization is viable**: He survives by not competing head-to-head with Siemens
3. **Canadian market is insufficient**: Despite building a world-class product, he needed international customers
4. **Institutional knowledge is currency**: He succeeds because he differentiates on thermal modeling, material science, and research depth
5. **OEM relationships require trust**: They came to him because of his credibility, not because he cold-pitched

Your platform is a software/ecosystem play, not hardware. But you'll face the same adoption curves, institutional barriers, and OEM dynamics Bob encounters.

The question isn't "Is the concept sound?" (Bob validates that). The question is: **"Can you survive a 3–5 year runway before reaching profitability, and can you expand beyond Canada to global markets?"**

If yes, you have a path. If you're constrained to Canada or need rapid profitability, rethink your strategy now.
