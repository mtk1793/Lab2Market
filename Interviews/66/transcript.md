**[00:00–02:00] — Opening / Intro**

**CK:** Happy to be here — I've had a lot of Microsoft account changes, three different enterprise accounts. Had to re-establish everything on my system. Anyway, here we are.

**MK:** Thank you for making it — I know you had a previous event running over. Let me introduce myself. My name is Mahmoud Kiasari, I'm doing my PhD at Dalhousie University. About two years ago we came up with an idea around additive manufacturing and supply chain — specifically digital inventory. I recently got accepted into a program called Lab2Market. In this program I have to talk with at least 100 experts like you to understand where the bottleneck is and whether there's a real place for our idea. It's still just an idea, not a product — I'm here to learn from you. Can you introduce yourself briefly?

---

**[02:02–03:32] — Clarifying the concept**

**CK:** So are you manufacturing something, or designing a digital platform?

**MK:** It's a digital platform. The software is the secret sauce. The idea is to build an ecosystem between different entities: OEMs, certification authorities and regulations (like DNV or Lloyd's Register), all built around 3D printing facilities. We want to encourage industries to install 3D printers on-site to produce parts locally — start small, small parts — and connect all these players together around digital inventory rather than physical inventory. We're also looking at integrating AI into the physical inventory management side because industries have been asking about that.

**CK:** Okay, so let me make sure I understand so I can give you useful insight. In your OEM example — I was a dealer for Daimler Trucks and Western Star. Parts were a nightmare. Are you talking about the digital platform for third-party component suppliers? Or the OEM directly? Because Daimler owned a lot of the pieces, but a lot were third-party components they designed and contracted out. As a dealer we'd want to be able to create parts on a need-to basis — eliminate freight costs, eliminate downtime, keep the client on the road.

**MK:** Yes. Our main focus is the end user — the industry that actually wants to use the product. We'd work with OEMs too, but they're going to be conservative and defensive about their IP.

---

**[05:16–08:23] — OEM resistance and market positioning**

**CK:** Honestly, if you go outside North America, you'll find less resistance. Daimler is a good example — they went back decades and designed their manufacturing so that if one location went down, two others could ramp up and meet demand. So when you find innovative manufacturers who have that "plan B" mindset, those are your people.

The cost argument is strong: with Western Star, truck owners needed the truck to make money — if the truck was down, they were losing. Equipment on those trucks was worth millions. Having a digital application tied to a 3D printer — that cost would pay for itself quickly.

On the commercial side — heavy duty trucks, highway stuff — the end users are the dealers. One-off shops could never handle the infrastructure of this; it won't be cheap. You could actually start a third-party business: people willing to collaborate — Mercedes parts, commercial vans, automotive. Dealerships and organizations could collaborate.

Here's the honest truth: Canadian SMEs are so far behind in technology it's frightening. People don't understand that the upfront cost saves them so much money down the road. They're so attached to their legacy systems they can't put anything new in — archaic, can't communicate data. I've seen it everywhere in WISC.

---

**[09:26–16:13] — Market segments and industry feedback**

**MK:** I've now spoken with around 60 people. Some say the idea is great, some prefer the old school way. One of the problems I face after the OEM question is procurement — I've spoken with DRDCs, shipbuilding companies, many others. They all know there's a problem. They know the cost of fixing it is less than the downtime cost. For some oil and gas companies, a day of lost operations costs $500K to $1 million. But no one moves — they just say "we have the money to absorb it."

**CK:** That's the oil and gas trap. Companies come in, make big bucks, think the well will keep filling. There are only a few who think long-term. Canada is such a broad, diverse, far-apart country — we need these manufacturing tools just to expand. Though the telecommunications infrastructure still needs work before digital inventory can operate everywhere seamlessly.

Anyway, the oil and gas guys: don't count on them exciting you. They don't think beyond the moment. There are good ones — but it is a challenge. I was at an event where the message was: "If we don't start supporting Canadian ideas and backing them with incentives, we'll keep giving it away."

**MK:** Completely agree. This idea exists in Europe and the US already — those markets are well ahead of Canada. The idea grew partly out of COVID — Canada realizing it can't rely on China for supply chain when a disruption hits. On aerospace — I've talked with Reaction Dynamics and Galaxy (satellites, not planes) and they love it. Also Air Ventures and Talis. Canada is increasing defense spending significantly. Our idea also covers parts no longer in production — you can scan them, add them to digital inventory, produce on demand.

**CK:** Aerospace — when you say that, I was at a dinner last night and there was a procurement person from WestJet and one from Havelin Air. Their comment: "We can't be down, we can't be waiting for parts." Boeing is coming in. You can't keep an inventory of an entire plane. There might be a real opportunity there. And Lufthansa Technik has their shop here in Calgary now as well. Their margins are so tight that what you're describing would be very beneficial.

Also: educational institutes. Trade schools. If you want to learn to fix a plane or an engine, you need one — but schools can't afford a full engine. By the time manufacturers donate outdated equipment, the technology has moved on. Having on-demand parts for training and tactile experience would be valuable. It's slow going with government funding, but those are down-the-road opportunities.

---

**[22:35–30:12] — Certification, OEM IP, and platform design**

**MK:** One thing I found: GKN (UK, commercial aircraft) told me to "not come near them." Their procurement and certification requirements are so strict for additive manufacturing — they said it's not mature enough. They can't guarantee a 3D-printed part won't put 300–400 lives at risk.

**CK:** Nothing is ever fully guaranteed. But this is where your design needs to include regulation from day one — build it in. Don't worry about aviation right now; design the product so that when you get there, the certifications can slot in. Work with companies like Havelin: "We know you have requirements. We're not saying we've met them. If we could meet them, would it be viable for you?" You need them to say "yes, we'd use it" — then figure out what they need, so you can design it in. Nothing should stop you from building toward that.

Transport Canada governs aviation approvals here. I just listened to the Minister of Transport — they are dying to support initiatives and technology. They know they have to move things forward. So co-develop the regulatory pathway. Have Havelin work with you to push the government side.

**MK:** That makes sense. I was also surprised by that one GKN response — one person's view isn't the whole organization.

On regulatory bodies — have you heard about Polyunity? They're doing the same concept but in healthcare/medical devices. I spoke with their co-founder Stephen Ryan, and they offer potential collaboration. He said if I grow, we could work together. Right now my original focus was oil and gas, but I'm shifting.

Also — Lloyd's Register and DNV. Those are the two big names in oil and gas certification. I pitched them. They liked the idea — not loved, but liked. They warned me their 7-step certification process is strict. But it's a starting point.

**CK:** Have you reached out to NextGen Manufacturing Canada?

**MK:** Not yet.

**CK:** Connect with them. They're already plugged into these guys. Everything has to go through checks and balances — but I'm kind of surprised everyone is jumping up and down saying "no we can't do this." Because everything has to. It's not unique to your idea.

What I'm gathering from the resistance is that they feel like they're going to lose something — IP protection instinct. But I'm thinking: they don't make all the pieces, they just put them together. If they could have the pieces more efficiently, when things are down...

**MK:** Exactly. Our platform model is: OEM shares a portion of the blueprint, their end user can print a small part locally. OEM gets 90–95% of the revenue. We take a small platform fee. The end user is happy — no supply chain problem. OEM is happy — more revenue. I don't understand where the resistance is.

---

**[33:25–50:46] — AI, digital adoption, and the "tomato analogy"**

**MK:** On AI — I've found companies like Enbridge have massive physical inventories and want to know if AI can help manage them more efficiently. Our next platform phase covers physical inventory management using AI. Do you think that's valuable or just a nice idea?

**CK:** Have you done any statistical analysis of technology awareness in Canada?

**MK:** Not yet.

**CK:** So — manufacturing in Canada is 20 years behind on software upgrades. Legacy systems like Oracle and SAP are crippling companies; they just keep patching. Data is scattered, it doesn't cross properly internally. And here's the number: only **14% of Canadian companies are currently using AI**. The US is around 58–60%. Mexico is almost 40%. Canada is at 14%.

People don't understand how to use AI tools. They shut it down, they don't make it available, they're afraid to pay for isolated cloud services. There's AI for contract review, for data mapping — people don't even know what Copilot can do inside their own enterprise. They're afraid of it. And either way it's coming for them — whether they engage or not.

**MK:** I agree completely.

**CK:** The bigger problem no one has solved: taking legacy software data and cleaning it so it can actually be migrated into a better application and you can get real inventory visibility. I haven't seen any startup that's been able to do that. SAP consultants come in and say "you have a data problem" but they don't fix it — they just say that.

Here's the real AI use case that no one's cracked — and I call it the "tomato problem": One alternator, same part, from ten different manufacturers, with ten different part numbers, all entered differently across different internal systems. Your inventory system says you have 10 of 8 different things. You actually just have tomatoes. AI needs to go in and recognize: these are all the same item, map them together, give me one normalized record. I need 10 tomatoes — I don't care what you call them.

Result without this: overstock on some variants, stockouts on others, obsolescence on everything. And it's even worse internationally — different languages, different part numbering conventions, different procurement systems.

**MK:** That's a fantastic analogy. I'm going to use that. From oil and gas to tomatoes — supply chain is all connected.

**CK:** That's supply chain. Everything is intertwined. People just don't see it until they do.

Also — there's a new international law coming into force: companies must be able to prove their supply chain from raw material to end user is free of child labor and forced labor. Large companies are going to push this down to their SME suppliers — "if you don't have visibility and traceability, we can't deal with you." 95% of Canada is SMEs. They think it won't affect them. It will. And if you make a part locally, you only need to verify raw materials — you've solved most of the compliance problem.

**[54:13–01:00:03] — Referrals and closing advice**

**MK:** One quick favor — I always ask at the end. I still have about 40 more conversations to go. Do you have any people in your network who might be helpful?

**CK:** Yes — but I'm going to reword how you ask them. Because of some of the rejections you've received, I think the pitch needs adjusting. These people are tech-savvy and innovation-minded. The ask should be: "If this platform existed and met all the regulatory/safety requirements, would you be interested in using it?" That's the question. Then — "if yes, then you should talk to this person." Don't lead with the problems. Lead with "if it was ready, would you want it?"

I'm going to reach out to the aerospace contacts. And there are a few other organizations.

**MK:** That's a fantastic pitch reframe.

**CK:** Don't worry about oil and gas. You want organizations with an innovative mindset to be your early adopters. Leaders who test it, validate it. Oil and gas will get there eventually — but everyone who supports oil and gas could be using your platform first.

**MK:** That's a really good point. Final advice for me?

**CK:** Do you have anyone handling the technology side, or is it just you?

**MK:** Just me right now. Applying for NRCC IRAP and other funding. Trying to build a team. Right now I'm still in customer discovery — figuring out which markets are real, then moving toward funding and team-building.

**CK:** Halifax has IdeaHOP at Dalhousie, which is good. But come out to Platform Calgary sometime — the startup ecosystem here is much more developed. Also look at AMII (Alberta Machine Intelligence Institute) out of U of A — they were among the top AI research groups in North America. Those are the people who can give you real guidance.

And here's my last piece of advice: you're 32. You think you started late. I'm going to be 63. You know when you're late? When they cremate you. If you do one piece more every day than you did yesterday, that's better than nothing. And if you don't go anywhere, you don't get anything.

**MK:** Thank you so much. That was a genuinely great conversation.
