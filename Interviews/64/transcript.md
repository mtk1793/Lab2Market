# Interview Transcript — Dean Dalpe (Interview #64)
**Interviewee:** Dean Dalpe  
**Position:** Governance & Operations Excellence, Enbridge  
**Background:** 32 years in utility sector (power generation, gas utility)  
**Date:** March 9, 2026  
**Interviewer:** Mahmoud Kiasari

---

## TRANSCRIPT

**Mahmoud:** Thank you for making time. I'm doing a PhD at Dalhousie and recently got accepted into the Lab2Market program. I'm validating an idea related to supply chain management using additive manufacturing and AI. Would you introduce yourself?

**Dean:** LinkedIn doesn't capture my background well because I don't use it much. I've spent about 32 years in the utility sector—power generation and gas utility. The first 15 years I worked in the field, then moved up through management. I started very technical and project-focused, got bored, and moved into leadership. I ran four power plants for TransAlt Energy and various parts of the business for Enbridge. Right now I have overall governance, bringing together utilities from operational and execution standpoint across North America. I deal with contract, supply chain, execution, operation standards, and how operations manages their work.

I'm probably the least popular person in an organization because I go in and dissect operations thoroughly. I look under every stone to show what's not working—people don't like that. But I do it to drive value. We've been very successful in Ontario, pulling 20–25% efficiency out of the organization by finding inefficiencies people thought were efficient.

**Mahmoud:** I like brutal and honest feedback. Can you walk me through spare parts availability in your current role? With 30+ years of experience, what does the critical spare parts situation actually look like today?

**Dean:** I can speak from both gas utility and electric utility. When we talk about critical spare parts, we're talking about anything that's critical. If you think about a key section of pipeline with damage, you need the same type of pipe that meets standards—it can't sit in a farmer's field. 

For gas transmission, you have turbines, compressors. You need to ensure critical spares are there. You have to assess through a risk matrix and determine: "Am I willing to pay for that product to sit on the shelf?" But people forget—if it sits there 10 years with seals, it's no good to you.

You're trying to balance: Do I need a critical spare, or do I make agreements with suppliers to hold critical spares and pay a fee? You end up paying more for the product, but you don't carry it. You don't manage its lifecycle. You don't have to worry about it disappearing. You have a contract with penalties if they don't deliver.

We test this all the time—yearly audits, confirming they actually have parts available.

**Mahmoud:** That's comprehensive. Let me ask about physical vs. digital inventory management. Across Enbridge's distribution network covering thousands of assets—how do you manage this inventory? Is it centralized or regional?

**Dean:** In Ontario, it's centralized through supply chain, but with governance. You want someone in operations to have accountability and sign off on any inventory changes. So if we change parts, it's up to the regional director and VP accountable for that area.

We have accountability for supply chain contracts. They're accountable for the bottom line of operations, reliability. You have to have that strong governance because supply chain people come and go. They don't always have operational experience. They just look at costs. You need operators involved who understand the real impact.

**Mahmoud:** Before going deeper, I want to know your perspective: Have you considered additive manufacturing or 3D printing for supply chain management?

**Dean:** Can you explain what you mean by additive?

**Mahmoud:** The idea is shifting from physical inventory to digital inventory. Instead of stocking a part physically on-site, you'd produce it on-site using 3D printers when needed. For smaller parts that help keep operations running. After COVID, Canada realized it can't rely on China or other countries due to borders being closed. We came up with this idea to help reduce downtime costs—you need a part urgently but have to wait weeks.

**Dean:** The acceptance will be an old-school mindset vs. new-school mindset. The **quality concern is paramount**—the exact integrity of the product being produced. Yes, you can get it at lower cost, quicker, but quality has to be insured. X-rays for metal, composition testing for plastics. In the gas industry, the big thing is avoiding failure.

We test products in heat and cold to confirm exact composition from existing suppliers. How do you know a 3D printed part meets the same criteria until you've actually printed one and tested it? That's the problem. You'd have to prove it and manage it. Are you doing it on a large scale across many products, or one product at a time? Sometimes value is diminished if it's just one product.

**Thinking 10 years out:** Could technologies create products stronger and higher quality using 3D printing? Absolutely. Then I think it has the ability. I need a product and someone prints it, or my company has the materials.

The problem is: Do you carry so many different products to be able to do that? I want to put pipeline and maintain pipeline. I'd have a set contract that you build these types of products.

**Mahmoud:** Thank you. My final core question: From your supply chain expertise, how could AI help inventory management? You mentioned needing to interpret high volumes of data—what's your perspective on using AI?

**Dean:** We use ChatGPT for all our manuals. Google can't do this. A guy in the field asks, "How do I do fusion on a 2-inch plastic pipe in Toronto?" It comes back: "Based on what you're saying, here's the manual section—click here." **That's the kind of interpretation where ChatGPT differs from Google.** People confuse them because they don't know how to use AI.

I use Genesis, Perplexity, and others. I'm one of the operational people highly connected to IT, looking at advancing AI. We're looking at all our planning and scheduling and seeing how we can use AI. **The problem is we have to make sure our backend data is coordinated so it can pull consistently—not overly random.**

It's not about me not trusting it; **it's people using it thinking it should do everything for them out of the blue.** There are limitations. You have to educate people on what it can and can't do. You have to educate them on where there are flags, when it doesn't make sense.

This is a new learned learning for people vs. old learning where you do math and run spreadsheets. Now you can descend it upright and it does it, but you have to ask it lots of questions to make sure it's hitting the mark. **Communication with AI is complex. People say it didn't give me the right answer. Well, you didn't ask it right.**

**Mahmoud:** One final concern: Trust. Many countries and universities restrict uploading company/university data to public AI due to security and cybersecurity concerns. Do you use local AI or general AI?

**Dean:** It's all local and filtered—stays within the company. I use both—one for myself (public), one for the company (very limited). The local one is slowing us down immensely to advance as quick as we want because of the security limitations. I understand the security piece—we're a large company and people try to get into our system all the time. There's money to be made if they steal or prevent us from seeing our own information.

**Mahmoud:** That's all my core questions. Can you share your thoughts on what I should focus on?

**Dean:** Managing inventory optimally is critical. In a company like Enbridge, why do I need to carry the same product across different regions? If Montreal only goes through 2 per year, maybe we should only carry 4 across the company in a central warehouse shifted 24 hours.

Also, **when products are retired, you should be able to write them off at the right time instead of huge warehouses full of stuff that's never moved in 20 years.** Products don't move because people don't know what they were used for or what they can be used for going forward.

**Training engineers on designing with available products:** If you already have a product that meets your design criteria and saves you $100K, use it instead of designing something new and buying another.

**Another supply chain challenge:** People continue to want to design very specific products. They want to re-engineer everything, which is inefficient. **Link design standards to purchasing.** Link engineering to supply chain. You have to manage engineering because they cost too much. Engineers say "we know best, this is what we need," but it's not always the most efficient way.

How do you bring design and supply chain together? Engineering relies on specific training and builds things in a specific way. Certain redundancies make costs too high. Therefore, when you build an asset, the overall project is too costly.

**From a supply chain perspective:** AI can help you know which products sit and never move but could be used elsewhere. AI has the ability to help. We just don't know how to use it very well yet.

**Mahmoud:** Thank you. Would you be willing to stay connected and have me reach back out as I progress?

**Dean:** More sure. I would love to help. We have a huge organization in India that writes all our AI. I can connect you with my IT guy, Eashwar. He's always looking at where AI can go and what it can do for our business.

One thing we did: We built a safety platform where field guides take a picture where they're working, and it highlights based on all codes in Ontario. Everything that's a potential risk gets circled. You don't have to do it all, but it highlights so your brain sees way more than it normally would. **You can't rely on it 100%, but it's an invaluable tool.**

We're evolving on the AI front—looking at how we can drive big value for quality and safety.

6. **Data coordination is prerequisite for effective AI implementation**
7. **People education on AI limitations is critical for adoption**

9. **Design-to-supply-chain linkage** reduces re-engineering waste
10. **AI is maturing but requires proof, governance, and human judgment**
