### 3. How do you decide when to make parts internally versus externally?

**Response:**
> "Based on the type of project and the potential they have to do it in the house or outside of the house."

**Make-or-Buy Decision Criteria:**

**Project Type Assessment** ðŸ“‹
- **Complexity:** Technical difficulty of manufacturing
- **Volume:** Batch size and production run length
- **Specifications:** Unique requirements or standard processes
- **Timeline:** Urgency and lead time requirements
- **Customer requirements:** Specific certifications or controls needed

**Capability Evaluation** ðŸ”§
- **In-house potential:** "What CAN we do internally?"
  - Equipment availability
  - Staff expertise and capacity
  - Material availability
  - Quality control capabilities
  - Cost competitiveness

- **External potential:** "What can OEM partners do?"
  - Specialized capabilities not available in-house
  - Cost advantages (scale, location, expertise)
  - Capacity during high-demand periods
  - Geographic proximity to customer
  - Certifications or accreditations

**Key Insights:**
- **Project-specific decisions** - No blanket rule, evaluated case-by-case
- **Capability-driven** - Based on realistic assessment of who can do it best
- **Pragmatic approach** - Considers both technical and business factors
- **Flexibility valued** - Having options provides competitive advantage

**Analysis:**

**Typical Make (In-House) Scenarios:**
- Proprietary or IP-sensitive components
- Very high margin products
- Small batch/custom work not cost-effective for partner
- Rapid turnaround required
- Quality control critical and difficult to outsource
- Core competency/strategic capability

**Typical Buy (External OEM) Scenarios:**
- Commodity or standard components
- Large volume production benefiting from scale
- Specialized processes not cost-effective to build in-house
- Capacity constraints during peak demand
- Geographic proximity to customer important
- Partner has unique equipment/certification

**Decision Framework (Implied):**

```
IF (in-house has capability) AND (cost competitive OR strategic importance)
  THEN Make
ELSE IF (OEM partner available) AND (meets requirements) AND (cost/time acceptable)
  THEN Buy
ELSE
  Evaluate: Build capability OR Find new partner OR Decline project
```

**Pain Point Identified:** ðŸŸ¡ Make-or-buy decisions require evaluation each time (manual, time-consuming)
**Opportunity Identified:** ðŸ’¡ Decision support tools with cost modeling and capability matching could streamline decisions

- 2x customers â‰  2x resources (could be 3x or 1.5x)

**Types of Changes During Scaling:**
- Process refinements based on bottlenecks
- Organizational structure adjustments
- Technology upgrades or automation

**Analysis:**

**Why Non-Linear Understanding Matters:**

**Common Scaling Failures:**
- Assuming linear cost scaling (reality: step functions)
- Under-investing in infrastructure/systems
- Hiring ahead of revenue (or behind demand)
- Quality degradation during rapid growth
- Culture/communication breaking down

**Reality vs. Fantasy in Scaling:**
- Fantasy: "We can 10x revenue with same team"
- Reality: New capabilities needed at each stage
- Fantasy: "Process that works now will work at 10x"
- Reality: Automation/systems needed for scale
- Fantasy: "Just need more customers"
- Reality: Operations, finance, support all must scale

- Stage 3: Mostly automated, 200 units/month
