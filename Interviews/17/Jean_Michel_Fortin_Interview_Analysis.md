## üö® **PAIN POINT #2: BREAKDOWNS (THE REAL KILLER)**

### **Predictive vs. Reactive Maintenance:**

> **Critical Quote:** "It's the breakdowns the problem. That's where your idea of 3D printing‚Äîit's when it breaks and it's not supposed to break, or when an accident happened. What do you do? That's where the real downtime happened."

**Two Types of Maintenance:**

1. **Predictive Maintenance (MANAGEABLE):**
   > "To me, predictive maintenance‚Äîwe know it right now. So it's easy to order the right parts, you know, to not stress the supply chain."
   
   - ‚úÖ Sensors installed ("We have a lot of sensors right now. We're collecting a lot of data.")
   - ‚úÖ Know when scheduled maintenance needed
   - ‚úÖ Can plan parts orders in advance
   - ‚úÖ **Not the problem**

2. **Breakdowns (THE CRISIS):**
   - ‚ùå Unexpected failures
   - ‚ùå Equipment breaking when it "shouldn't"
   - ‚ùå Accidents and unpredictable events
   - ‚ùå **THIS is where downtime happens**

### **Why Breakdowns Are Catastrophic in Arctic:**

**Normal Industry:** Breakdown ‚Üí Order part ‚Üí Ship overnight ‚Üí Fix next day  
**Arctic Industry:** Breakdown ‚Üí Part not in stock ‚Üí **Wait 12 months** or emergency airlift at massive cost

> "Sometimes we need to bring the parts by planes, by vessels."

**Emergency Logistics:**
- ‚úàÔ∏è Charter flights to Arctic (extremely expensive)
- üö¢ Emergency vessel charters (if season allows)
- ‚è∞ Multi-day delivery even with air freight
- üí∞ Cost of emergency logistics >> cost of part

**Downtime Cost:**
- Large mine stopped = $500K-$1M+ per day
- Short season (1.5-4 months) = every day critical
- Lost production cannot be recovered
- Investors/stakeholders impacted

## üéØ **HYPOTHESIS VALIDATION: AM/DIGITAL INVENTORY**

### **Current Status: NOT CONSIDERING AM**

**You Asked:**
> "Are you focusing on additive manufacturing? Is it part of your plan or no, is completely off the chart?"

**Jean-Michel's Answer:**
> "No, no, we're not working on it right now."

**Follow-up:**
> "Have you ever thought about moving to digital inventory rather than physical inventory?"

**Jean-Michel's Answer:**
> "No, not at all. Never thought about it. Might be a good idea if it works, yeah, that really works, maybe."

### **Why They're Not Considering AM:**

#### **1. Volume Challenge:**

> "Maybe another challenge, it's the volume because we're big, big mines. It takes a lot of material to make it happen."

**The Volume Problem:**
- Large-scale mining operations
- High-volume parts consumption
- AM = low-volume, high-mix (opposite of mining needs)
- Example: Conveyor belt rollers (thousands needed)
- Example: Crusher liners (massive, high-wear)

**AM Sweet Spot:** Small batches, custom parts, complex geometries  
**Mining Reality:** Mass production, simple but large parts, high-volume wear items

#### **2. Supplier-Certified Parts:**

> "Right now we're not dealing a lot with the regulation. That's where the regular parts we're using are coming from suppliers. So it's basically, it comes with the equipment we're using."

**Current Model:**
- Buy equipment from OEMs (Caterpillar, Komatsu, etc.)
- Parts come certified with equipment
- Supplier handles regulatory compliance
- **No internal certification burden**

**AM Would Require:**
- In-house certification process
- Internal quality control
- Regulatory approval for safety-critical parts
- **Adds complexity they don't currently have**

#### **3. Focus on Predictable, Not Emergency:**

Jean-Michel's perspective is that **predictive maintenance is already working**:
- Sensors installed
- Data collection in place
- Can forecast scheduled maintenance
- Can order those parts 12 months ahead

**The Gap:**
- AM doesn't solve the **planning problem** (still need to forecast)
- AM doesn't solve the **volume problem** (can't print thousands of rollers)
- AM **could** solve the **breakdown problem**, but they haven't connected those dots yet
