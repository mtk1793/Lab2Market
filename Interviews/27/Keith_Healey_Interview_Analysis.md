### **Q2: Energy Transition & Net-Zero Tension**

**Question Asked:**
> "From your work on the Sustainable Jobs Partnership Council and your time at Suncor, how do you see offshore operators managing the tension between extending the life of legacy platforms (2040-2050) while transitioning to net-zero?"

**Keith's Response (Key Points):**

1. **Net-Zero Timeline Was Unrealistic:**
   > "There's been a lot of pressure and hype with net-zero and renewables - that was a global movement, Paris Accords. But once the reality set in, the timeline was **not realistic** in terms of how fast we could get there. The world is still going to need oil - maybe not for burning fossil fuel for cars, but you still need it for **plastics, clothing, and other things we haven't found suitable replacements for yet**."

2. **Government Role in Transition:**
   > "It takes a huge capital investment to go to renewables, and companies are not willing to put money in when it's uncertain about demand. This is where government plays a role - like the $85 million just awarded for expanding charging stations across Canada. Those things have to be built first. You're not going to get an electric car company to come in and make that huge investment to create demand. **It has to be the government.**"

3. **US Policy Impact:**
   > "Where the US pulled back with the Trump government - their dislike of renewables and wanting to continue with fossil fuels - that really put a slowdown. Our economies are tied between Canada, US, and globally."

4. **How Oil Companies Navigate Tension:**
   > "East Coast oil and gas is **low-carbon oil and gas** just by the very nature of it. Making investments in technology that captures CO2 and other methane emissions - invest in technology that reduces harmful greenhouse gases. I think with the global demand, use of technology to reduce carbon footprint - that's how we navigate the tensions between the two different camps."

5. **Federal Government Realism:**
   > "I'm glad to see the current federal government sees a very realistic timeline - it's going to take longer to get there. The world still has consumption for oil and gas. Canada has abundance of this resource, and we **must well capitalize on it for prosperity to fund the transition**."

**Hypothesis Validation:**
- ‚úÖ **NH.27 SUPPORTED:** Energy transition creates demand for rapid asset adaptation (legacy platforms need to operate until 2040-2050)
- ‚úÖ **NH.30 SUPPORTED:** Net-zero commitments create budget for carbon-reducing supply chain innovations
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **POLITICAL CONTEXT:** Federal government is **pro-oil & gas** (not anti-fossil fuel) - frames transition as "decades, not years"

### **Q3: Regulatory Pathway & C-NLOPB Certification**

**Question Asked:**
> "You spent 7 years managing Canada-Newfoundland Benefits deliverables at Suncor. If a new technology like digital inventory wanted to serve offshore operators, what's the realistic certification pathway through C-NLOPB, and what are the biggest hurdles?"

**Keith's Response (Key Points):**

1. **Biggest Hurdle: Regulator's Limited Knowledge:**
   > "The biggest hurdle is **the regulator itself - their limited knowledge on technology**. That unfortunately makes the path longer. You could have a great technology that's revolutionary for offshore in reducing cost and enhancing safety, but the regulator is going to **put the brakes on** and say 'Hey, this is not traditionally how we do it. We want to see validation proof that this will be safe.'"

2. **Required Studies:**
   > "You have to spend more money on studies - impact studies, validation studies. You'll have to use organizations like **C-CORE** or whoever to validate models to show that what you're trying to do is safe and does not take away from existing safety to the environment."

3. **Education Process:**
   > "It's a whole education to the regulator. You would have to **invite them along** - invite them to milestone reports where the researcher presents and shows 'This is the study we did, these are the calculations, this is the outcome in terms of impacting the metal or whatever.' Over time, you'll need a certification authority to sign off like **Lloyd's** [and DNV]."

4. **Critical Success Factor:**
   > "You would need to get their sign-off, and that's when the regulator would feel comfortable. It'll be very important to **engage the regulator and certified authority along the way**. If you just go out and do the research and then pass it over to the regulator, they're gonna be like 'Ooh, we don't have people here that can really understand this. It's going to take us a year, maybe year and a half to get back to you.' **If you engage them on the way, you'll break down those barriers.**"

**Hypothesis Validation:**
- ‚úÖ **NH.7 SUPPORTED:** Certification is the primary gatekeeper (regulator's limited knowledge is the barrier)
- ‚úÖ **NH.28 SUPPORTED:** Regulatory compliance costs delay adoption (year+ review time if not engaged early)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **CRITICAL PROCESS:** Must engage C-NLOPB + DNV/Lloyd's + C-CORE **during R&D**, not after

**Strategic Implication:**
ERINL funding should include:
1. C-CORE validation studies
2. DNV/Lloyd's certification
3. C-NLOPB milestone presentations (quarterly updates)

**New Contact:** **C-CORE** (validation organization for offshore technology)

### **Q4: IP Management & OEM Collaboration**

**Question Asked:**
> "From your R&D advisory roles at ExxonMobil and Suncor, why are operators so protective of spare parts IP, and what would it take for an OEM to release part files to a secure digital platform?"

**Keith's Response (Key Points):**

1. **Operator is Caught in the Middle:**
   > "The operator is very concerned about **liability with an OEM**. If you got an OEM who makes specialized valves for an FPSO, and you were saying 'It'd be awesome if we can do additive manufacturing on that because it's a long lead order time. If we had the IP rights and blueprint, we could have that in a digital warehouse and only produce it when needed vs having spares on hand in a warehouse costing us money to mitigate corrosion, pay to keep it in shape, sitting on a shelf.'"

2. **OEM's Fear:**
   > "The tendency is with the OEM equipment suppliers - they see it as a **threat to a loss of business**."

3. **Operator's Fear:**
   > "Oil companies are afraid that if they cross the line in terms of broken an IP commitment and shared a file with an additive manufacturer, they're afraid it could have **legal consequences** and shut down relationships and their ability to have these valves or equipment shipped on time."

4. **Solution: Consortium Model:**
   > "I think what needs to happen is additive manufacturers need to **come together in a consortium, like a society**, and propose a secure digital warehouse and **negotiate a price on the IP for the blueprint**. The OEM manufacturers have to see 'Where's the money for me in this? I just can't give you my IP - I'm giving away my business.'"

5. **Business Case for OEMs:**
   > "They would have to economically find out: Right now companies are buying spares, and even companies side by side are probably buying the same spares but they don't know they have the same equipment. The OEM is making money on that. But **what if you made more money if you sold the digital file?** That's the business case that needs to be understood from additive manufacturers to pitch to the OEM. The OEM has to understand where's their business model, their path on this, how can they make money? They will want **commissions or payment not just for the license, but on everyone that is made**."

**Hypothesis Validation:**
- ‚úÖ **NH.10 SUPPORTED:** IP protection is a major blocker (OEMs see it as "loss of business")
- ‚úÖ **NH.31 SUPPORTED:** IP concerns are heightened in government-funded R&D (operators fear legal consequences)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **SOLUTION IDENTIFIED:** **Consortium model** + **Per-print royalty** (not just license fee)

**Strategic Implication:**
Revenue model must include:
1. **Upfront license fee** to OEM (one-time)
2. **Per-print royalty** to OEM (ongoing revenue stream)
3. **Consortium structure** (multiple AM providers, not single company)
