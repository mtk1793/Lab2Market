Central Hypothesis & Research Gaps
The foundational thesis driving the CAPSM framework, identifying 5 critical gaps in modern power system AI control.
Central Hypothesis
"A dual-process, metacognitively guided, physics-informed control architecture ‚Äî inspired by human cognitive science ‚Äî can simultaneously achieve sub-5ms fault response, near-optimal economic dispatch, >99.8% constraint compliance, and cyber-attack resilience in modern power grids with high renewable/EV penetration."
Research Gaps
G1

Single-Speed Control Paradigm

Existing AI controllers operate at a single temporal resolution ‚Äî either fast reflex OR slow optimization ‚Äî but never both integrated. No architecture combines sub-5 ms fault response with multi-minute economic optimization in a unified cognitive framework.
‚úì Addressed by: Dual-process System-1 / System-2 architecture with metacognitive arbitration
G2

Sample Inefficiency of Deep RL

Standard DQN/PPO require millions of episodes to converge in high-dimensional grid environments. Convergence is too slow for real-time adaptation during cascading failures or topology changes.
‚úì Addressed by: Quantum-Inspired RL (QIRL) with superposition-based exploration and tunneling
G3

Physics-Blind Neural Controllers

Most neural network controllers ignore fundamental power system physics (Kirchhoff's laws, AC power flow equations). This leads to unsafe control actions that violate voltage, current, and thermal limits.
‚úì Addressed by: Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) safety layer enforcing AC power flow constraints
G4

Scalability of Multi-Agent Coordination

Centralized RL does not scale beyond small test systems. Distributed approaches lack coordination, leading to oscillatory or conflicting control actions across regions.
‚úì Addressed by: Hierarchical Multi-Agent RL (HMARL) with regional agents and global coordinator
G5

Cyber-Physical Resilience Under Attack

Power grid control systems are increasingly vulnerable to cyber attacks (FDI, DoS, man-in-the-middle). Existing controllers have no integrated mechanism to detect and recover from coordinated cyber-physical attacks.
‚úì Addressed by: Metacognitive anomaly detection + CAPSM's dual-process fallback architecture

HIRL: Hierarchical Integrated Reinforcement Learning
HIRL unifies QIRL's quantum-inspired exploration with hierarchical multi-agent coordination (HMARL) into a single scalable RL framework that operates across multiple temporal and spatial resolutions. It bridges System-2's QIRL optimizer with the HMARL scalability layer, creating a two-tier hierarchy.
Key Innovation
Regional agents use QIRL policies locally while a global coordinator merges strategies using federated policy aggregation ‚Äî enabling seamless scaling from 9-bus to 300+ bus systems without retraining from scratch.
